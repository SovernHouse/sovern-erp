**Last Updated:** 2026-04-17

Different from the CMO content skill (which governs marketing content for inbound) — this skill governs cold one-to-one outreach.

---

## I. Why Cold Outreach Copy Is Its Own Discipline

Cold email copy is not marketing copy. It's not nurture copy. It's not sales-deck copy.

It is a **single-purpose artifact:** earn 15–30 seconds of a stranger's attention and convert to a reply. That's the only job.

Everything else — the 30-year story, the QC expertise, the 5% commission, the case studies — comes *after* the reply. If the cold email tries to do all of that at once, it gets deleted.

The three hard rules:
1. **60–125 words.** Research across 3M+ cold emails (2026) shows this range delivers 2.4× the reply rate of emails > 200 words. Elite performers average < 80 words in first-touch.
2. **One ask.** Not two. Not "reply or book a call or download this." One specific next action.
3. **Personalization is non-negotiable.** Generic templates with `{first_name}` reply at 2–3%; emails with a real, specific, researched opening line reply at 10–18%.

## II. The Sovern House Voice

### Voice principles

- **Direct, not salesy.** Say what you mean in plain sentences. No "I hope this finds you well."
- **Confident without bragging.** The founder story is the proof; the copy doesn't need to oversell it.
- **Respectful of the reader's time.** The shorter the email, the more respect it signals.
- **Specific.** Numbers, product categories, countries, Incoterms — concrete beats abstract every time.
- **Lower-cased when natural.** Alex's emails can open "hi [name]" rather than "Dear Mr. [Surname]" for US/Canada/UK/AU. Keep Mandarin / Taiwanese / LATAM contacts more formal.
- **British-English-leaning.** Alex is Canadian but the brand sits globally. "Optimise," "colour," "centre" is fine; avoid heavy US idioms that don't travel.

### Voice anti-patterns (never write these)

- "I hope this finds you well" / "Hope you're having a great week"
- "My name is X and I work at Y" (we know; it's in the from-line)
- "Quick question" as a subject line (overused; spam flag)
- "Hop on a call" (US tech idiom; doesn't travel)
- "Circle back" / "touch base" / "jump on a quick call"
- "Revolutionary" / "game-changing" / "best-in-class" / "world-class" / "industry-leading"
- "Just checking in" (kills the cadence)
- "I know you're busy so I'll keep this short" (performative; shut up and be short)
- Exclamation marks (any more than one and it reads as hype)
- "Dear Sir/Madam" — you didn't research the prospect

### Voice anchors to use

- "30 years on the ground in Asia"
- "Mandarin and Taiwanese fluency" (never just "I speak Chinese")
- "5% flat commission, no hidden spread"
- "We negotiate directly with the factory owner"
- "We're your buying office in Asia"
- "Your eyes and ears in Taiwan / China"
- "QC is built into every deal, not an afterthought"
- "No translation risk, no hidden markup"
- "You see the factory price, you pay factory + 5%"

## III. The Email Architecture

Every Sovern House cold email follows this structure:

```
Subject:      3–6 words, specific, looks like a human wrote it

Opener:       1 sentence. A specific, researched observation about THEM.
              Trigger event, recent news, a job change, a product launch.
              Proves you're not a bot.

Context:      1–2 sentences. Why you, why now. Pick one value-prop anchor.
              Do not list credentials — weave them into the context.

Ask:          1 sentence. One CTA, specific.
              "Worth a 15-minute call next Tuesday?"
              "Should I send a short note on how we'd approach Taiwan sourcing?"
              "Open to a reply with your current lead times?"

Signature:    Name, role, company, phone, 1 trust signal
              (e.g., "New Route International Exchange, Taiwan")

Legal footer: Physical business address (Taiwan) + one-line unsubscribe link.
              Required under CAN-SPAM (US), CASL (Canada), GDPR (EU).
              Do not skip — see trade-email-deliverability.md section XI.
```

Word count target: 60–90 words for first-touch. 40–60 for follow-ups (signature + legal footer don't count toward body-copy word budget).

**Legal footer pattern — use on every cold send:**

```
—
Alex [Surname] · Sovern House
[Street Address, City], Taiwan
Sovern House is a brand of New Route International Exchange
Unsubscribe: [tracked-link] · Prefer LinkedIn? [linkedin url]
```

The unsubscribe link is automatically inserted by Smartlead/Instantly when configured — confirm it's live before any send. Bare-minimum compliance: address + unsubscribe mechanism. Without both, the send is a regulatory violation in the US and CASL-breaching in Canada regardless of our copy quality.

### Subject line principles

**Works:**
- Question format: "Taiwan flooring sourcing?"
- Mutual context: "via [mutual contact]"
- Observation: "your Panjiva imports"
- Curiosity with specificity: "5% vs. your current agent"
- Lower-case when natural: "quick taiwan sourcing question"

**Doesn't work:**
- "Quick question" (generic, overused, spam-flagged)
- ALL CAPS anywhere
- "URGENT" / "Important" / "Action Required"
- Emojis (fine for consumer B2C, suspicious for B2B trade)
- Over 50 characters (truncated on mobile)
- "RE:" or "FW:" fakeouts (buyers will flag you and you deserve it)

### The opener is the email

If the first line is generic, the email is dead on arrival. Good first lines come from:
- A Panjiva/ImportGenius record showing a specific recent shipment
- A LinkedIn post they've made in the past 30 days
- A press release, new-hire announcement, or product launch
- A trade show they attended
- An industry report they're quoted in
- A specific pain point implied by their public activity

Bad first lines:
- "I noticed your company is in the flooring business..." (not specific; every agent says this)
- "I hope you're doing well..." (dead on arrival)
- "My name is..." (it's in the from-line)

## IV. Email Templates by Vertical

All templates below are **starting points**, not sends. Every email must be personalized on the opener before it goes out.

### Flooring — US wholesaler (general Taiwan/China sourcing)

```
Subject: Taiwan flooring sourcing

Hi [Name],

Saw [Company] has been bringing in [product type] from [origin country]
on the [vessel/quarter] Panjiva records — the volume looks like real
business, not tire-kicking.

We're a buying house based in Taiwan, 30 years on the ground. We source
flooring direct from factories in Taiwan and China, handle QC, handle
documents, 5% flat on FOB. No hidden spread.

Worth a 15-minute call on how we'd quote your next flooring order?

Alex
Sovern House — your buying office in Asia
Sovern House is a brand of New Route International Exchange (Taiwan)
```

**Why it works:** specific Panjiva reference proves research; direct positioning of the buying-house model; transparent fee eliminates the #1 trading-agent fear (hidden spread); one ask with a time frame.

### Flooring — US wholesaler (Malaysia LVT/SPC campaign)

**Campaign context:** Sovern House has a direct factory relationship with a Malaysian LVT/SPC manufacturer. Pitch this as factory-direct access, not as a sourcing intermediary. Never say "I have an agreement" or "I work with a factory" — speak as if the factory is ours. The tariff angle (zero Section 301 vs. 25%+ on Chinese-origin) is the hook; DDP is the closer.

**Positioning rules for this campaign:**
- Say "we're shipping from our factory in Malaysia" — not "I have an agency agreement" or "there's a factory I work with"
- Lead with what THEY get: factory-direct pricing, Malaysia-origin certificates, zero Section 301
- Offer FOB or DDP — DDP removes all logistics risk from the buyer, which is a strong closer for companies that haven't imported direct before
- Never use "buying house" framing for this campaign — it implies a middleman layer that doesn't exist here

```
Subject: Malaysia LVT/SPC for [Company]

Hi [Name],

[One specific sentence about them — their company history, their import
volume, their product line, or a recent public move. Proves you're not
a bot.]

We're shipping LVT/SPC from our factory in Malaysia directly to US
distributors. Malaysia-origin certificates, zero Section 301 vs. 25%+
on Chinese-origin, specs built for the US market. We ship FOB or DDP —
DDP means it lands at your warehouse with duties and freight fully covered.

Worth 15 minutes to see if the pricing works for [Company]?
```

**Why it works:** positions as factory-direct, not middleman; tariff gap is specific and quantified; DDP removes the last objection (import complexity); one clear ask.

## V. The Follow-Up Sequence

Recall the 9-touch cadence from `trade-sales.md`. Here are the follow-up copy templates.

### Touch 2 (Day 4) — value add

```
Subject: re: Taiwan flooring sourcing

Hi [Name],

Different angle — Taiwan flooring exports into the US are up 14% YoY,
and the factories I work with are taking on new Western buyers for the
first time in a decade.

If that changes the picture, happy to walk through options on a call.

If not, I'll close the loop.

Alex
```

**Rules for Touch 2:**
- Do not say "just bumping this up" / "did my last email not go through?"
- Do not resend the first email with a new subject
- Offer new information, not a re-ask
- 40–60 words max

### Touch 3 (Day 10) — reframe

```
Subject: the thing most importers miss on Taiwan sourcing

Hi [Name],

Most buyers coming to Taiwan for the first time assume prices are higher
than mainland China. They usually aren't — once you account for QC
rejection rate, tariff exposure, and lead-time reliability, Taiwan often
wins on landed cost.

Worth a 15 minutes on how we'd quote a comparison for [product]?

Alex
```

### Touch 4 (Day 18) — short check-in

```
Subject: re: Taiwan sourcing

Hi [Name],

Is this a priority this quarter or should I circle back next?

Alex
```

Yes, really. Two lines. Check-in emails this short outperform longer
ones — they respect the reader and the reply bar is absurdly low.

### Touch 5 (Day 25) — breakup

```
Subject: closing the loop

Hi [Name],

I'll stop reaching out on Taiwan sourcing. If anything changes —
particularly if [trigger: tariff change, quality failure, supplier
drop — pick one specific to them] — my door's open.

Alex
Sovern House
```

**Why breakups work:** they remove implicit pressure, read as confident
(not desperate), and regularly generate 3–7% reply rate from prospects
who needed to see you respect their "no."

### Touch 6+ (Quarterly nurture)

One email per quarter. NOT a newsletter. NOT "just checking in."

Send a genuinely useful piece of content or observation:
- A specific tariff update affecting their category
- A short market note: "Taiwan flooring prices moved ~5% last quarter, here's why"
- A public case study: "Here's how we structured a [similar-sized importer]'s first Taiwan order"
- A specific factory-level observation: "Three Taiwan flooring factories we work with are taking new buyers for the first time in 2 years"

Never more than 100 words. Always one specific, useful thing.

## VI. LinkedIn Copy

### Connection request (with 200-char note)

```
Hi [Name] — saw your [post / company / role]. I run a Taiwan-based
buying house, 30 years in-region, specialise in [their category].
No pitch, happy to connect.
```

Accept rate jumps from ~20% (blind) to ~60%+ with a specific note.

### InMail / DM after connection accepted

```
Thanks for the connect, [Name].

Quick context — 30 years sourcing out of Taiwan, Mandarin speaker,
flat 5% buying commission. Saw [Company] is importing [product] —
if you're ever open to a second set of eyes on a quote, my door's open.

Not asking for a call; just putting a hand up.

Alex
```

### LinkedIn reply-to-post (highest-intent channel)

When a prospect posts something relevant (e.g., "frustrated with our current overseas supplier"), reply in comments with one short, helpful observation — no pitch — then DM them the pitch 24–48h later. Shows up as a warm touch, not cold.

## VII. Reply Handling Copy

### "Interesting, tell me more"

```
Hi [Name],

Happy to.

Short version: we're based in Taiwan, 30 years in Asian sourcing,
Mandarin and Taiwanese fluent. We source direct from factories for
clients like [anonymized similar-profile client], handle QC on every
run, manage the paperwork, and charge a flat 5% of the FOB factory price.
No hidden spread.

For [their company type], the typical starting point is a trial sourcing
project on a single SKU — low commitment, lets you see how we work.

Calendar: [link]. Or reply with 2–3 times that work for you this week
and I'll send an invite.

Alex
```

### "What do you charge?"

```
Hi [Name],

Flat 5% of FOB factory price on standard sourcing. We show you the
factory price, you pay factory + 5%. No hidden markup.

Smaller orders or private-label programmes (design, sampling, packaging)
run higher — typically 7–10% — because of the fixed sourcing overhead.
Principal trades where we buy-and-resell are 8–15% on spread, quoted
transparently.

Happy to walk through a specific scenario if you share a product and
rough volume.

Alex
```

### "Send me a brochure" / "Send me info"

Do NOT send a brochure. Send this:

```
Hi [Name],

No brochure — we work deal-by-deal, so a PDF wouldn't tell you much.

Better: send me one product you're sourcing today (type, rough volume,
current origin), and I'll send you back a short note on how we'd
approach it, with a ballpark price and lead time. 48 hours.

Alex
```

### "How do I know you're real?"

```
Hi [Name],

Fair question — important one.

Things we can do to answer it:
- Taiwan business registration number + New Route International Exchange
  filing — happy to send.
- Live video call from the Taiwan office, in Mandarin or English.
- Reference call with a current customer sourcing [similar product].
- Factory video tour once we narrow down which factory makes sense
  for your order.

Which of those would move the needle for you?

Alex
```

### "We already have an agent"

```
Hi [Name],

Totally understand. Most buyers we work with already had an agent
before us.

Curious — what's working well with your current setup, and what do
you wish was better? No commitment; if the honest answer is "everything's
fine," I'll stop reaching out.

Alex
```

### "Not interested"

```
Hi [Name],

Understood — I'll close the loop.

If anything changes, particularly [one specific trigger relevant
to them], my door's open.

Alex
```

One email. Move to quarterly nurture. Do not re-engage immediately.

## VIII. Word-Level Patterns

### Strong verbs

Prefer: source, negotiate, inspect, ship, verify, quote, close, handle, manage, run, route, land.

Avoid: leverage, utilize, synergize, optimize, facilitate, drive, enable.

### Strong nouns

Prefer: factory, container, shipment, Incoterm, FOB, DDP, sample, inspection, certificate, tariff, port, bill of lading, spec sheet.

Avoid: solution, platform, ecosystem, framework, methodology.

### Numbers beat adjectives

Not: "We have extensive experience sourcing from Taiwan"
Yes: "30 years sourcing out of Taiwan"

Not: "Highly competitive pricing"
Yes: "5% flat FOB, nothing on top"

Not: "Fast turnaround"
Yes: "Sample in 14 days, production in 45"

### Sentence length

Vary. Short punchy sentences. Then one longer sentence that shows you can think and write at length, giving the paragraph rhythm. Then another short one.

## IX. Localization for International Outreach

### US / Canada

- Casual opening acceptable ("Hi Bob,")
- Direct ask is expected
- First-name basis usually fine

### UK

- Slightly more formal ("Hi James" or "Dear James")
- Avoid over-direct CTAs; soft-close better received
- "Happy to" / "open to" lands better than "let's do"

### EU (DE, NL, FR)

- More formal: "Dear Mr. Müller"
- Germans reward specificity and precision; include numbers, dates, specifications
- French / Italian buyers respond to relationship framing more than technical spec

### Latin America (Portuguese/Spanish-speaking)

- Relationship-first — open with a warmer acknowledgment
- Mention language capability: "Happy to continue in Portuguese/Spanish if easier"
- Don't rush the ask in the first email; warm touch first, ask second

### Asia (CN/TW/JP/KR/SE Asia)

- Use Mandarin/Taiwanese for CN/TW prospects when Alex is sending (major advantage — deploy it)
- Formal: "Dear Mr./Ms. [Surname]"
- Indirect CTAs preferred — "If it is convenient, perhaps a short call at your convenience"
- Recognize regional holidays (Chinese New Year, etc.) in timing

### Middle East

- Formal salutation always
- Relationship and reputation signals weigh heavily
- Business hours Sunday–Thursday in most markets — adjust send timing

## X. Quality Gates — Before Any Email Sends

**Self-review checklist:**

- [ ] Is the opener specific to this exact prospect? (Not just their industry.)
- [ ] Would I send this to a friend who ran this company, or is it "mass email" voice?
- [ ] Can I cut 20% of the words without losing meaning? (Usually yes.)
- [ ] Is there exactly one ask?
- [ ] Is the ask specific (date, length, format)?
- [ ] No hype words? (revolutionary, game-changing, world-class, etc.)
- [ ] No "I hope this finds you well" / filler openers?
- [ ] Does the signature contain one trust signal (Taiwan-based, 30 years, etc.)?
- [ ] Word count between 60 and 125?
- [ ] No attachments?
- [ ] Link count ≤ 1?
- [ ] mail-tester.com spam triggers: clean?
- [ ] Sanctions screening status: cleared?

## XI. What the Copywriter Blocks

**Never approve for send:**

1. An email with a generic opener (`I came across your company...`)
2. An email over 150 words on first touch
3. Any email with multiple asks
4. Any email with "I hope this finds you well" / "touching base" / "circling back"
5. Any email with attachments on cold outbound
6. Any email with more than one link
7. Any email with ALL CAPS or multiple exclamation marks
8. Any email promising capability we can't deliver
9. Any template that hasn't been personalized per-prospect
10. Any email with hype words (revolutionary, best-in-class, world-class, game-changing)
11. Any email sent before sanctions screening is complete (L-013)
12. Any email sent from sovernhouse.co primary domain for cold outreach

**Flag for review:**
- Any new vertical template before first campaign launch
- Any subject line variant that hasn't been tested
- Any reply template used > 10 times without iteration
- Any email longer than 150 words in first touch
- Any proposed automation that strips personalization
