# Sovern House — Email Rules, Reasoning & Institutional Memory

**Version:** 1.0 | **Last Updated:** 2026-04-25
**Applies to:** Every outbound email sent under the Sovern House name — to suppliers, buyers, prospects, and partners.
**Read before:** Writing, drafting, or sending any email via the ERP or any other channel.

---

## PART 1 — HARD RULES (Non-negotiable. Apply to every email, every time.)

### R-001 — Alex approves every email before it sends

No email is sent until Alex has seen the full draft (subject line + body) in the chat and given explicit approval. "Send it," "yes," or equivalent. No exceptions for any recipient type — supplier, buyer, cold prospect, or partner.

**Why:** Emails sent autonomously in April 2026 contained language that implied order readiness and revealed pipeline information that damaged our negotiating position. The ERP is a send tool, not an autonomous agent.

---

### R-002 — Never request a PI, sales note, or any order-stage document without explicit instruction

A Proforma Invoice (PI), commercial invoice, sales confirmation, or order sheet is only requested when Alex explicitly says to. These documents signal intent to buy. We are not ready to buy until Alex says we are.

**Why:** An email to Reena (Frontech) included "please prepare a Proforma Invoice for 2,000 sets" — we had no client, no confirmed order, and no instruction from Alex. This was incorrect and premature.

**In practice:** End supplier information-gathering emails with a neutral close: "We will revert once we have completed our evaluation." Never "please prepare" or "we are ready to proceed."

---

### R-003 — Never reveal our sales or client pipeline status to any supplier

We have no confirmed clients. We have sent outreach. We have had no replies from buyers. None of this is shared with any supplier. We are a buying office evaluating suppliers for our markets. That is the only framing suppliers need.

**Why:** Suppliers adjust pricing, urgency, and treatment when they know a buyer has no confirmed orders. Revealing our status removes all negotiating leverage before a deal even begins.

**In practice:** Never say "we're just getting started," "we have an interested buyer," "no orders yet," or anything that places us at a disadvantage. Maintain confident professional neutrality.

---

### R-004 — Never use language that implies order readiness unless instructed

Prohibited phrases in supplier emails:
- "We are ready to move quickly"
- "We want to move to a formal quote"
- "We are keen to move forward"
- "Please proceed"
- "We will confirm the order"
- "Next steps toward purchase"
- "We are close to placing an order"

Permitted supplier closing language:
- "We will revert within 48 hours of receiving your quote."
- "We will come back once we have completed our review."
- "We will follow up once we have the full information."
- "Thank you — we will be in touch."

---

### R-005 — ERP signature is automatic. Never sign an email with Alex's name.

The ERP injects the full signature block automatically. Never end an email body with "Alex," "Alex McConnell," "Best regards, Alex," or any name. The last line of the email body is the last line of the message content.

**Why:** This has been corrected four or more times. It creates a duplicate name in the delivered email (body name + ERP signature name).

---

### R-006 — No em dashes anywhere

Zero em dashes (—) in any email body, subject line, or template. Use periods, commas, colons, semicolons, or parentheses instead.

**Why:** Alex's explicit preference, captured 2026-04-24.

---

### R-007 — BCC Fanzey on all Egypt outreach emails

Every email sent to an Egyptian buyer, prospect, or distributor must BCC: Mohannad Fanzey `mohanadfanzey@gmail.com`.

**Why:** Fanzey is Sovern House Egypt Country Manager based in Zagazig. He needs visibility on all Egypt communications. This rule stays until Alex explicitly lifts it.

---

### R-008 — No attachments on cold outreach

Cold emails to buyers carry no attachments. Attachments trigger spam filters and create friction. If the prospect asks for a brochure, send a link to sovernhouse.co or a tailored short reply (see template in Part 4).

---

### R-009A — Never state a specific fee, percentage, or price without Alex's approval

We do not have a fixed commission rate. Fees are agreed per client and per deal. Never quote a percentage (e.g. "5%"), a fee structure, or a price range in any buyer or supplier email without Alex explicitly confirming the number first.

**Why:** Locking a percentage into a reply before a deal is scoped gives away negotiating room and may be wrong for the client's actual volume and product mix.

**In practice:** When a buyer asks "what do you charge?" — write the draft reply with a placeholder where the fee goes, present it to Alex in chat, and edit the fee together before sending. Never fill in a number yourself.

---

### R-009 — Sanctions screening before any new contact receives an email

Every new prospect must be screened via the compliance process before the first email sends. Do not add to ERP and send in the same action without confirming screening is complete.

## PART 2 — SUPPLIER COMMUNICATION RULES

Suppliers (factories, manufacturers, trading companies in Asia) are a fundamentally different audience from buyers. The rules are different.

### Framing with suppliers

We are a buying office acting on behalf of importers in our target markets. We do not reveal who the specific end client is unless we have a confirmed deal. Standard framing:

> "We are a buying office in Asia sourcing on behalf of an auto parts distributor in North Africa."

This framing:
- Establishes legitimacy (we are a professional intermediary, not a random buyer)
- Protects the client's identity (supplier cannot go around us to the buyer directly)
- Maintains price neutrality (supplier does not know the end margin or urgency)

### Information-gathering emails to suppliers

Goal: Get pricing, product specs, certifications, carton dimensions, MOQs, and lead times. Nothing more at this stage.

What to ask for:
- FOB price (never accept EXW as final — if they quote EXW, follow up requesting FOB)
- Carton dimensions (L x W x H cm), gross weight per carton, units per outer carton
- MOQ per SKU and per order
- Lead time
- Sample availability and cost
- Certifications (CE, RoHS, ECE R90, etc. as relevant)
- Payment terms

What not to say:
- Do not ask for a PI until Alex says to
- Do not mention the end buyer name or country in a way that gives them the ability to contact the buyer directly
- Do not say "we are ready to move quickly"
- Do not give a quantity commitment before you have one

### Supplier close language (correct)

> "We will revert within 48 hours of receiving your quote."
> "We will follow up once we have reviewed the full cost model."
> "Thank you — we will be in touch."

### When a supplier quotes EXW

EXW (Ex Works) prices are useless for landed cost calculations. Always push back:

> "Could you please requote on FOB [nearest port] terms? We need a clean FOB figure to calculate freight costs for our client."

If the supplier cannot provide FOB directly, ask for:
- Domestic freight cost from factory to port (per unit or per CBM)
- Export clearance/documentation charge

## PART 3 — BUYER AND PROSPECT COMMUNICATION RULES

### Stage 1: Cold outreach (no prior relationship)

**Goal:** One reply. Not a deal, not a meeting, not even a call. One reply.

Rules:
- 60-125 words for first touch. No exceptions.
- One ask only.
- Personalized opener — specific observation about THEM (not their industry, not "I came across your company").
- No attachments.
- No brochure. No company overview. No PDF.
- No "I hope this finds you well." No "quick question" subject lines.

**Stage reality:** We have no clients yet. Do not reference other clients. Do not claim volume. Do not make promises about timeline.

### Stage 2: Reply received (prospect is interested)

This is the most important moment. The prospect has replied. Do not blow it.

Rules:
- Reply within 24 hours.
- Be specific. Give one concrete next step.
- Do not send a brochure ("no brochure — we work deal by deal").
- Ask them one question that moves the qualification forward: what product, what volume, what's their current sourcing setup.
- Do not negotiate price. That is a later stage.

### Stage 3: Qualification call / ongoing exchange

- Understand their pain (current supplier problems, QC failures, tariff issues, cost pressure)
- Understand their product (specs, certifications needed, volumes)
- Understand their timeline (is there a live need or are they exploring?)
- Do NOT push. Trust > speed. An importer choosing a sourcing partner is a 30-90 day decision.

### Stage 4: Formal quote / PI

This stage only begins when Alex explicitly says to proceed. At this stage:
- A PI from the supplier is requested (only now)
- A quote to the buyer is prepared (only now)
- Commission is disclosed transparently

## PART 4 — MARKET-SPECIFIC RULES AND REASONING

### United States

**Tone:** Personal. First-person founder framing. Casual but precise.

**Why personal framing ("I'm the founder..."):** US buyers respond to the founder story. A personal email from a 30-year veteran reads as authentic; a corporate "we" reads as a mass mailer. The US is the most competitive flooring import market — personality differentiates.

**Critical: Never lead with China for US buyers.**

US buyers face Anti-Dumping / Countervailing Duty (AD/CVD) tariffs on flooring from China. Leading with China in a US email immediately signals they will face tariff exposure. For US buyers, lead with: Taiwan, Vietnam, Thailand. China can be mentioned but not as the primary or first origin.

**Why:** The subject line "LVT from Taiwan — no AD exposure" was written specifically to address this. Taiwan and Vietnam are the clean-origin story for US buyers. This is not about hiding China — it is about leading with what solves their actual problem (tariff avoidance).

**Opening style:** "hi [Name]," (lowercase, casual — feels human, not corporate)

**CTA style:** "Worth a 15-minute call next Tuesday?" — direct, specific, time-bounded.

### UK and EU

**Tone:** Slightly more formal than US. "Hi [Name]" acceptable. Soft-close preferred.

**CTA style:** "Worth a reply with your current lead times?" (soft, low-commitment ask)

**EU-specific:** Mention CE certification, REACH compliance. German buyers want specs and numbers. French and Italian buyers respond to relationship framing.

### Latin America (Portuguese/Spanish)

**Tone:** Formal. "Dear team," or "Dear [Name]," Works better than first-name casual.

**Language:** Always offer to continue in Spanish or Portuguese. Use both in the same email if you don't know which.

**CTA style:** "Kindly advise a suitable time for a call this week." (formal invitation, not a casual ask)

**Why "Kindly":** LATAM business culture is more formal than US/AU. The "Kindly" framing signals respect and professionalism. It was chosen and kept after observing that Chilean, Colombian, and Mexican templates performed better with this tone.

### Egypt and North Africa (Arabic-speaking markets)

**Tone:** Formal. "Dear [Name / Team],"

**Pain point framing:** Egyptian auto parts importers deal with specific documentation burdens — CoC (Certificate of Conformity), pre-shipment inspection, certificates of origin. Name these specifically. It proves you understand the Egyptian import environment, not just generic Asia sourcing.

**Fanzey mention:** Mention our local Egypt representative where appropriate — it closes the distance gap and increases trust. "We also have a local representative in Egypt if you'd prefer to speak with someone nearby first."

**Why:** Egypt buyers are buying from Asia into a complex customs environment. Our ability to handle documentation at origin, and to have a boots-on-the-ground local contact, is a stronger value proposition than price alone.

**BCC rule:** Always BCC mohanadfanzey@gmail.com on every Egypt email. See R-007.

### Middle East (non-Egypt)

**Tone:** Formal. "Dear [Name]," Business hours: Sunday-Thursday.

**No casual CTAs.** Use: "Kindly advise a suitable time."

### China / Taiwan suppliers

**Tone:** Professional. "Hi [Name]," or "Dear [Name],"

**Language advantage:** Deploy Alex's Mandarin fluency as a signal of credibility, not just a feature. "I'm the founder of Sovern House — 30 years on the ground, Mandarin-fluent, negotiating direct with factory owners" lands differently in supplier communication than "we speak Chinese."

**China sensitivity:** Never mention tariffs, AD/CVD, or geopolitical risk in supplier emails. This is a buyer-side concern, not a supplier-side conversation.

## PART 5 — TEMPLATE REVISIONS LOG (What we changed and why)

This section captures actual edits made to templates after they were first drafted, with reasoning. This is institutional memory — read it before writing new templates.

### Egypt Auto Parts — First Touch

**Original draft included:** Generic opening without pain-point specificity.

**Revised to include:** Specific Egyptian customs documentation pain ("CoC, pre-shipment inspection, certificates of origin — all falling on the importer to chase"). This was added because Egyptian importers have a specific, well-known pain point with Egypt's import documentation requirements that most Asian suppliers do not address proactively. Naming it specifically signals that we understand their market, not just generic sourcing.

**Added line:** "We also have a local representative in Egypt if you'd prefer to speak with someone nearby first." — This was added to reduce the distance/trust gap. Egypt buyers are skeptical of Asia-based suppliers they cannot meet. Fanzey gives us a local point of contact that no other buying office in Asia has.

### US Flooring — Asia Sourcing (Touch 1)

**Key revision:** Changed from "We source from China and Vietnam" to "We source LVT and SPC from certified factories in Vietnam, Thailand, and Taiwan."

**Why:** US buyers face AD/CVD tariff exposure on China-origin flooring (anti-dumping duties on LVT and SPC). Leading with China as a sourcing origin immediately raises the tariff flag. Vietnam, Thailand, and Taiwan are all currently lower-tariff origins for US buyers. China should not be the headline — it can appear later in conversation once the relationship is established and the buyer's specific exposure is understood.

**Subject line "LVT from Taiwan — no AD exposure"** was written specifically for US buyers who are actively re-evaluating their supply chain due to AD/CVD risk. This subject line speaks directly to their burning problem.

### Garments / Basics — Americas

**Key decision:** Use "Kindly share your volume targets and categories" as CTA rather than "Worth a call?"

**Why:** Garment buyers in the Americas are sourcing-team buyers, not CEO-level decision-makers as often as flooring. They respond better to a direct action request (share specs) than a vague social ask (let's talk). Getting them to share product specs is a lower-commitment action than booking a call, and it moves the deal forward more efficiently.

### Supplier RFQ Emails (LED Headlights, Crystal DGLS, NAOEVO)

**Key framing decision:** "We are a buying office in Asia sourcing on behalf of an auto parts distributor in North Africa."

**Why:** This framing:
1. Establishes that we are a professional buying office (credibility)
2. Does not name the specific distributor (protects the client relationship)
3. Explains the geography (helps the supplier understand certification and compliance requirements)
4. Implies volume is coming from a real buyer (not just a spec inquiry)

**What we do NOT say:** We do not say "our client is Fanzey in Zagazig" or name any individual. We do not say we have a confirmed order. We do not reveal our pipeline stage.

### Frontech Brake Pad Emails (Reena)

**Good emails in this thread:** Carton dimensions request, FNH number clarification, freight clarification — all correctly framed as information-gathering.

**Mistake captured as L-002/R-002:** The email that included "please prepare a Proforma Invoice for 2,000 sets" was sent without Alex's approval and before any order was confirmed. This is the specific pattern that R-002 is designed to prevent.

**What the Reena emails did well:** Specific questions, numbered clearly, professional tone, no filler, no pressure. This is the correct supplier communication pattern for information-gathering stage.

## PART 6 — STANDARD REPLIES (When a buyer responds)

### "Interesting — tell me more"

> Hi [Name],
>
> Happy to.
>
> Short version: we're based in Taiwan, 30 years in Asian sourcing, Mandarin-fluent. We source direct from factories for importers across [their region/vertical], handle QC on every run, manage the paperwork, and work on a transparent fee basis agreed upfront. No hidden spread.
>
> For your situation, the typical starting point is a trial sourcing project on one SKU or category — low commitment, lets you see how we work before scaling.
>
> [Calendar link / offer 2-3 times for a call]

### "What do you charge?"

**Process: Draft the reply, flag Alex to review and edit together before sending. Do not send without his sign-off.**

Draft starting point (Alex edits the fee before it sends):

> Hi [Name],
>
> [FEE TO BE CONFIRMED WITH ALEX BEFORE SENDING]
>
> You see the factory price, you pay factory + our fee. No hidden markup, no spread.
>
> Happy to walk through a specific product and volume if you want a concrete number.

**RULE:** When a buyer asks what we charge, write the draft, bring it to Alex in chat, and edit the fee together. He confirms the number. Then it sends. Never fill in a percentage or fee amount yourself.

### "Send me a brochure / more information"

Do NOT send a brochure. Send this instead:

> Hi [Name],
>
> No brochure — we work deal by deal, so a PDF wouldn't tell you much.
>
> Better: send me one product you're sourcing today (type, rough volume, current origin) and I'll send back a short note on how we'd approach it, with a ballpark price and lead time. 48 hours.

### "We already have an agent"

> Hi [Name],
>
> Totally understood. Most buyers we work with already had an agent before us.
>
> Curious: what's working well with your current setup, and what do you wish was better? No commitment — if the honest answer is "everything's fine," I'll stop reaching out.

### "Not interested"

> Hi [Name],
>
> Understood — I'll close the loop.
>
> If anything changes, particularly [one specific relevant trigger], my door's open.

One email. Move to quarterly nurture. Do not re-engage for 90 days.

## PART 7 — WHAT WE NEVER SAY (reference list)

### In buyer emails
- "I hope this finds you well"
- "Just checking in" / "Just following up"
- "Touching base" / "Circling back" / "Looping back"
- "Quick question" (as a subject line)
- "Hop on a call" (US tech idiom, doesn't travel internationally)
- "Revolutionary" / "game-changing" / "world-class" / "best-in-class"
- "We're the best buying office in Asia"
- Any claim we cannot immediately back up with a specific fact

### In supplier emails
- Any mention of our current pipeline status
- Any mention of specific client names without authorization
- "We are ready to move forward" / "We are keen to proceed"
- "Please prepare a PI / proforma invoice" (without Alex instruction)
- "We are close to placing an order"
- Any language that creates artificial urgency or commitment we do not have

### In all emails
- Em dashes (use period, comma, colon, semicolon, or parentheses instead)
- Alex's name at the end of the body (ERP signature handles this)
- Exclamation marks (maximum one per email, only if genuinely warranted)
- Any fabricated client names, volumes, or references
- Any specific fee percentage, margin rate, or price without Alex's explicit confirmation first

## PART 8 — EMAIL STAGE REFERENCE

| Stage | Who | What we say | What we never say |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cold outreach — buyer | Prospect with no relationship | Specific opener, one ask, 60-125 words | Pipeline status, client names, order volumes |
| Follow-up — buyer | No reply yet | New info or insight, not a re-ask | "Just following up," "did you see my last email" |
| Reply handling — buyer | Interested prospect | Qualify, one next step, no brochure | Price negotiation (too early), PI (too early) |
| Qualification — buyer | Engaged prospect | Understand their product, volume, timeline | Pressure, urgency, commitment |
| Information gathering — supplier | Factory or manufacturer | Pricing, specs, certs, carton dims, MOQ | PI request, order commitment, client identity |
| Clarification — supplier | Existing supplier contact | Specific technical question | Multiple questions at once (number them), order pressure |
| PI request — supplier | Confirmed by Alex only | Formal PI request with full specs | Anything that wasn't confirmed by Alex |
