## 2. Rigid-Core Resilient: SPC vs WPC vs LVT vs IronLite Core

### Composition and Density

| Type | Core composition | Density | Feel underfoot | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **SPC (Stone Polymer Composite)** | PVC + ~60-70% limestone (CaCO₃) + stabilisers | ~2,050 kg/m³ | Hard, like ceramic tile. Hollow acoustic. | Maximum dimensional stability, dent resistance, click integrity. The mass-market workhorse. |
| **WPC (Wood Plastic Composite)** | PVC + wood flour + calcium carbonate + foaming agent | ~1,100 kg/m³ | Soft, springy, warm. Closer to real engineered wood. | Quiet residential rooms, multi-family where acoustic ratings matter, premium "feel" demands. |
| **LVT/LVP (Luxury Vinyl Tile/Plank)** | Solid or semi-flexible PVC | ~1,400 kg/m³ | Slightly flexible, requires near-perfect subfloor. | Glue-down commercial (healthcare, retail). High design fidelity. |
| **IronLite Core** | SPC wear + WPC middle + SPC stability, three layers | 1,200 kg/m³ | Walks like engineered hardwood. Warm, not hollow. | The "best of both" engineered solution: SPC durability with WPC warmth, 41% lighter than SPC. |

### Why the Three-Layer Sandwich Wins

A single-layer SPC core puts all the work on one material. A single-layer WPC core is too soft for high-traffic locking. IronLite Core separates the functions: SPC handles wear and stability, WPC handles acoustic warmth and click stress dampening, IXPE handles substrate noise. **The sandwich is the story.** No single-layer rigid core does all three jobs without compromise.

## 3. Wear Layer to Use-Case Mapping (Sovern's Qualifying Lines)

Wear layer is the transparent PVC top film. It is the only thing between foot traffic and the print layer. Get this wrong and the floor fails inside 24 months regardless of core quality.

Measured in **mil** (one thousandth of an inch) for US-spec sheets, **mm** for EU/metric.

| Wear layer | Mil | Sovern qualifies for... | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **0.15 mm** | 6 mil | Light residential only. Bedrooms, closets, low-traffic. | DIY tier. Do not quote for any rental, multi-family, or commercial programme. |
| **0.3 mm** | 12 mil | Standard residential, single-family. Living rooms, hallways, kitchens. | Most-quoted residential spec. Acceptable for homeowner-occupied. **Wrong for landlords and multi-family.** |
| **0.5 mm** | 20 mil | Commercial-grade. Multi-family, light commercial, hospitality guest rooms. | **Sovern's default IronLite Core spec.** The line between "warranty-safe" and "warranty-risk" in any rental scenario. |
| **0.7 mm** | 28 mil | Heavy commercial. Corridors, retail floor, F&B, education. | Premium tier. Quote only when buyer specifies commercial use. |
| **1.0 mm+** | 40 mil+ | Heaviest commercial. Healthcare, transport hubs, high-traffic retail. | Specialist tier. Sovern can quote but factory lead times extend. |

**The qualifying question every flooring buyer should be asked first:** "What is this floor going into?" Apartment, hotel, office, hospital, retail, school. Quote the wear layer to match the answer, not the budget. Quoting under-spec is the single most common cause of flooring failure claims.

## 4. Standard Plank Dimensions

### Sovern Default

**180 mm x 1220 mm** (approximately 7.09" x 48"). This is what FlorWay and HanHua run by default on every IronLite Core SKU. Quote this dimension unless the buyer explicitly requests a different size. Breaking dimension means breaking the production line and adds lead time and cost.

### Industry-Standard Plank Sizes (US Market)

| Dimension | Metric equivalent | Market position |
|---|---|---|
| 6" x 36" | 152 x 914 mm | Older residential standard. Phased out for most new programmes. |
| **7" x 48"** | **178 x 1220 mm** | The classic US residential default. Closest imperial match to Sovern's 180 x 1220 mm. |
| 9" x 60" | 228 x 1524 mm | "XL plank" residential premium. Visually wider. Higher waste factor. |
| 7" x 60" | 178 x 1524 mm | Hybrid wide-and-long format. Hospitality and high-end residential. |
| 12" x 24" (tile format) | 305 x 610 mm | LVT tile look-alike for commercial and wet areas. |

### EU and Metric Markets

EU buyers expect metric specifications. 180 x 1220 mm reads naturally to them and matches their click-tool calibration. The Sovern default speaks both markets fluently. **Always state dimensions in both imperial and metric in any quote** because buyers convert in their head and round-off errors create disputes.

## 5. Locking Systems

Three patent holders own the click-lock industry. Every other "system name" is a licensed variant.

### The Three Patent Houses

| Patent holder | Key systems | Method | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Unilin (Mohawk)** | Uniclic | Angle-angle plus tap-down (flat-tap). Pre-tensioned tongue pulls planks tight. | Resilient subfloors and minor unevenness. Industry workhorse. |
| **Välinge** | 2G (angle-angle), 5G (drop-down with plastic insert), 5Gi (improved water-resistant 5G), Liteback | 5G uses a flexible glass-fibre-reinforced tongue that springs into the short-side groove on drop-down. | Fast install on perfect subfloors. The favourite of professional installers because it cuts install time. |
| **I4F (Innovations 4 Flooring)** | 3L TripleLock, Click4U, i-Click4U | Drop-lock on both sides, no inserts. One-piece moulding. | Maximum speed install. Most aggressive pricing for licensees. |

### Sovern's Default System

**Drop-down click.** Standard across the IronLite Core SKU range. Do not lead with this detail in marketing copy: buyers care about the result (fast, secure install) more than the brand of the licensed mechanism. Volunteer the system name only when the buyer asks specifically or when the spec sheet requires it.

### Internal note for the assistant

Some buyers will ask whether a click system is patented or licensed. The honest answer for IronLite Core is: drop-down click profile, no public Uniclic/Välinge/I4F licence claim. If pressed, defer to "we will confirm the licence status with the factory" rather than guess.

## 6. Finishes and Surface Treatments

### Resilient (LVT/SPC/WPC/IronLite Core)

| Finish | What it is | When to specify |
|---|---|---|
| **UV-cured PU (urethane)** | Standard topcoat. Polyurethane cured by UV light at the factory. | Almost every resilient floor on the market. The default. |
| **UV ceramic-bead PU** | UV PU with ceramic micro-beads suspended in it. | When buyer wants improved scratch resistance without specifying mil. |
| **Super anti-scratch UV** | Higher-bead density and harder PU formulation. | Sovern's IronLite Core finish. Survives stiletto heels, dog claws, dragged chairs. Use this phrase verbatim. |
| **Embossed-in-register (EIR)** | Surface texture aligned to the printed wood grain. Visual and tactile realism. | Premium residential and hospitality. Adds 5-15% to cost depending on plate complexity. |

### Engineered and Solid Hardwood

| Finish | What it is | When to specify |
|---|---|---|
| **Aluminium oxide UV** | Microscopic Al₂O₃ particles embedded in 6 to 10 coats of UV-cured urethane. | Hardest factory finish available. Standard on premium engineered hardwood. Resists scratch but difficult to refinish on site. |
| **UV oil** | Hardwax or natural oil cured by UV. Penetrates the wood rather than sitting on top. | Premium European look. Easier to spot-repair, requires recoating every 3-7 years. |
| **Site-finished polyurethane** | Liquid PU applied on site after install. | Custom restoration and high-end residential. Sovern does not typically supply site-finished. |
| **Swedish (acid-cure)** | Two-part conversion varnish. Hardest site-applied finish. | Restoration and gym/dance floor projects. Rare in import programmes. |

### Texture Treatments (Both Wood and Resilient)

| Texture | Effect | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Smooth** | Flat factory surface. | Modern, contemporary, retail premium. |
| **Wire-brushed** | Surface scoured with metal brushes to expose grain on softer wood; resilient mimics this with EIR. | Active homes with pets and kids. Hides accidental scratches. |
| **Hand-scraped** | Surface scraped to create rustic, undulating texture. | Country and rustic aesthetic. Excellent at hiding wear. |
| **Distressed** | Heavier scraping plus simulated wormholes, gouges, age marks. | Reclaimed and historic aesthetic. |
| **Smoked** | Wood reacted with ammonia to darken the tannins through the board, not just the surface. | Premium European engineered hardwood. |

## 8. Test Standards and Certifications

The assistant should be fluent in these references. They are the language flooring buyers respect.

### Performance Tests (ISO and EN)

| Standard | What it measures | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **ISO 23999:2021** | Dimensional stability (shrinkage) under heat. | Predicts seam separation. IronLite Core: 0.10% horizontal vs WPC industry-typical 0.20%. |
| **ISO 24343-1:2007** | Residual indentation under sustained load. | Predicts dent recovery after furniture loads. IronLite Core: 0.07 mm vs SPC 0.08 mm and WPC 0.09 mm. |
| **ISO 24338:2014** | Abrasion resistance of laminate (Taber-type abrasion). | The basis of laminate AC ratings. Method A and Method B. |
| **EN 660-2** | Abrasion test for resilient floor coverings (Frick-Taber). | EU equivalent for LVT/SPC durability claims. |
| **EN 425** | Castor chair resistance. | Office and healthcare specification. |
| **EN 13329** | The full laminate flooring performance standard suite (Europe). | Sets the AC class definitions. |
| **NALFA LF-01** | North American laminate flooring performance (ANSI-accredited). | The US equivalent to EN 13329. Used by NALFA-certified products. |

### AC Ratings (Laminate Abrasion Class, EPLF/EN 13329)

| AC class | Use level | Equivalent commercial spec |
|---|---|---|
| **AC1** | Light residential (bedrooms, closets). | Avoid for any rental. |
| **AC2** | General residential. | Acceptable for moderate-traffic owner-occupied. |
| **AC3** | Heavy residential, light commercial. | The mass-market default. |
| **AC4** | General commercial (offices, small retail). | Hotel guest rooms, light retail. |
| **AC5** | Heavy commercial. | Department stores, high-traffic corridors. |
| **AC6** | Industrial. | Specialty applications, rare in residential. |

### Slip Resistance

| Standard | Test method | Pass threshold |
|---|---|---|
| **ASTM D2047** | James Machine, static coefficient of friction. | ≥ 0.50 for "slip resistant" rating. |
| **ASTM E303** | Pendulum test (BPN). | PTV ≥ 36 typical for level floors. |
| **DIN 51130** | Inclined ramp test (R rating, R9 through R13). | R9 dry, R10 minor wet exposure, R11+ wet/oily. EU and global commercial spec. |

### Fire Rating

| Standard | What it measures | Common pass spec |
|---|---|---|
| **ASTM E648** | Critical Radiant Flux (CRF) for floor coverings under radiant heat. | Class I (≥ 0.45 W/cm²) for hospitals and high-traffic public buildings. Class II (≥ 0.22 W/cm²) acceptable for most commercial. |
| **ASTM E662** | Smoke density. | NBS specific optical density ≤ 450 for most commercial fire codes. |
| **EN 13501-1** | Reaction to fire (EU). | Cfl-s1 typical for residential resilient; Bfl-s1 for commercial. |

### Formaldehyde and VOC Compliance (Mandatory for US Import)

| Standard | Jurisdiction | What it requires |
|---|---|---|
| **CARB Phase 2 ATCM** | California mandatory, US de facto standard. | Composite wood components ≤ 0.05 ppm (HWPW), ≤ 0.09 ppm (PB), ≤ 0.11 ppm (MDF). |
| **TSCA Title VI (40 CFR 770)** | US federal (EPA). | Functionally identical to CARB Phase 2. Required on all composite-wood imports. Certificate of compliance and labelling. |
| **EPA TSCA Section 13** | All chemical-substance imports. | Customs entry compliance, not a flooring-specific test. |
| **ASTM D6007** | Small-chamber formaldehyde emissions test (the test method behind CARB/TSCA). | Reference method for laboratory certification. |
| **CDPH Standard Method v1.2** | California indoor-air emission spec. | Required for LEED, WELL, and many state-procurement programmes. |
| **FloorScore (SCS)** | Third-party VOC certification. | Buyer-facing badge. Widely requested in US commercial. |

### Janka Hardness (Solid and Engineered Hardwood Only)

Pound-force required to embed a 0.444" steel ball halfway into the wood. Higher = harder.

| Range | Examples | Suitability |
|---|---|---|
| **< 900 lbf** | Pine, alder, walnut | Soft. Hides scratches in rustic finish but dents easily. |
| **900 to 1,500 lbf** | Red oak (1,290), white oak (1,360), maple (1,450) | The residential workhorse range. |
| **1,500+ lbf** | Hickory (1,820), Brazilian cherry (2,820), Brazilian walnut/ipe (3,680) | Premium hardness. Justify with marketing. |

## 9. Tariffs and Trade Compliance (US Focus)

### The Three Stacking Layers on Chinese-Origin Resilient

When a Chinese resilient floor arrives at US Customs in 2026, three duties stack:

1. **Most Favoured Nation (MFN) base duty.** Around 3% for resilient flooring under HTS 3918.
2. **Section 301 China-specific tariff.** 25% on List 3 categories that include rigid-core resilient.
3. **Reciprocal/IEEPA tariff stack.** Additional rates depending on the current executive proclamation.

For Sovern's HanHua origin, the working US duty figure is **40.8%** combined. Always quote landed cost with this stack, never with MFN only.

### Friend-Shoring and Origin Optionality

The CBP anti-circumvention enforcement zone now covers Vietnam, Thailand, Malaysia, Indonesia, and Mexico. The agency's posture: products manufactured in China and minimally processed elsewhere still face Chinese tariff rates. Genuine substantial transformation is required.

**Sovern's Malaysia origin (FlorWay Sdn. Bhd., Port Klang) is genuine substantial transformation.** FlorWay manufactures the IronLite Core from raw inputs in Malaysia, not from Chinese semi-finished boards. This is what makes the 15.5% US duty defensible. Form D documentation from FlorWay holds up at USCBP.

Vietnamese and Thai SPC programmes that source semi-finished SPC from China and laminate or click-mill in Vietnam/Thailand are at high CBP risk in 2026. Several have been retroactively reclassified. The assistant should mention this when defending Sovern's Malaysian origin as the clean US-bound choice.

## 10. Common Flooring Defects (How to Diagnose, How to Talk to a Buyer)

Roughly 70% to 90% of flooring "defect" claims turn out to be installation or environmental on inspection, not manufacturing. The assistant should know the difference and frame buyer conversations accordingly.

### Resilient (SPC/WPC/LVT/IronLite Core)

| Defect | Visual | Most likely cause |
|---|---|---|
| **Cupping** | Edges higher than centre, U-shape. | Moisture from below (subfloor) or excess humidity. Subfloor moisture barrier missing or failed. |
| **Crowning** | Centre higher than edges, inverted U. | Drying after over-wet install, or excessive surface moisture. |
| **Peaking / Tenting** | Two boards meet and lift at the joint. | Installation too tight to walls or fixtures. No expansion gap. |
| **Gapping** | Open seams between planks. | HVAC humidity too low post-install. Inadequate acclimatisation. Subfloor settling. |
| **Edge lift / Curl** | Plank corners or edges raise off subfloor. | Subfloor flatness out of tolerance (typically > 3/16" over 10 ft). Loose adhesive on glue-down. Heat exposure for resilient. |
| **Telegraphing** | Subfloor seams, screws, or unevenness show through the resilient surface. | Subfloor preparation failure. Almost never a flooring defect. |
| **Click failure / Joint breakage** | Locking tongue snaps, planks separate. | Rolling load on a floating click floor (a flooring rule, not a defect). Or installation mallet on uncushioned subfloor. Or under-spec click profile for the use case. |
| **Delamination** | Wear layer separating from core, or core layers separating. | Manufacturing bond failure. Genuine warranty issue. Photograph the failure and document batch number. |
| **Photo film bubbling** | Print film appears to blister under wear layer. | Heat exposure or manufacturing bond defect. Inspect adjacent boards. |

### Engineered Hardwood

| Defect | Visual | Most likely cause |
|---|---|---|
| **Cupping** | Edges raised. | Subfloor moisture (typical) or HVAC moisture entering from below. |
| **Dry cupping** | Edges raised but subfloor is dry. | Indoor relative humidity below manufacturer spec (typically < 30% RH). Lamination shrinks at different rate to core. |
| **Endlift / Ski** | Plank ends curve upward. | Core moisture imbalance perpendicular to face. Often acclimatisation failure. |
| **Telegraphing** | Knots, voids, or filler from core show on face. | Core manufacturing defect, ply void, or thin face veneer. Real warranty case. |
| **Squeaking / Crackling** | Audible noise underfoot. | Subfloor unevenness, fastener migration, or click-joint friction. |
| **Finish wear-through** | Aluminium oxide finish dulls in walking paths. | End-of-life signal, not a defect, unless within warranty period and traffic was within spec. |

### Laminate

| Defect | Visual | Most likely cause |
|---|---|---|
| **Edge swelling** | HDF core expands at seams from moisture exposure. | Spill not cleaned promptly, or wet-mop habit. Laminate is moisture-vulnerable at the seams unless waterproof-rated. |
| **Print wear-through** | Decorative paper shows wear in traffic paths. | Wrong AC class for the use. AC3 in a commercial space is the most common version of this complaint. |
| **Click failure** | Same pattern as resilient. | Same causes as resilient. Often subfloor flatness. |
| **Pattern repeat noticeable** | Same plank graphic appears every 4 to 6 boards. | Low-end laminate has a short print drum. Premium laminate uses 20+ different print plates per box. Not a defect, just spec. |

### Solid Hardwood

| Defect | Visual | Most likely cause |
|---|---|---|
| **Cupping** | Edges higher than centre. | Moisture from below. Subfloor moisture barrier missing or basement venting. |
| **Crowning** | Centre higher than edges. | Post-installation surface moisture (wet-mop habit) or sanding wet floor. |
| **Gapping** | Open seams. | Low indoor humidity in heating season. Often resolves seasonally. |
| **Splinters / Slivers** | Surface fibre raises. | End-of-life wear pattern, or refinish failure. |
| **Refinish failure** | Site-finish bubbles, peels, or fails to bond. | Contamination of substrate, incompatible recoat product, or moisture during cure. |

### How the Assistant Should Frame Defect Conversations

When a buyer reports a problem:

1. Ask the use environment first. (Humidity range, HVAC schedule, subfloor type, install date.)
2. Ask whether the defect is on isolated planks or spread across the install.
3. Ask for photographs. Cupping vs crowning vs peaking are different problems and the photograph settles the argument.
4. Avoid promising "the factory will replace it" without Alex's explicit approval. Manufacturing defects exist but they are the minority of cases.
5. Default to "Sovern will investigate with the factory and confirm whether this is a warranty case" rather than agree or deny on the spot.
