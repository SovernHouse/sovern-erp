**Last Updated:** 2026-04-17
**Applies to:** All outbound prospecting, lead qualification, pipeline management, sales cadences, reply handling, and deal progression. Tuned to Sovern House's specific positioning — buying house, 5% flat commission, 30-year founder Asia story, flooring and garments as lead verticals.

## I. The Sales Philosophy — Read This First

Sovern House is a **buying house**, not a "vendor trying to close a deal." The entire sales motion flows from that identity. We sell on behalf of the buyer — we find them better suppliers, better quality, better prices — and we earn a transparent 5% commission. That framing changes everything:

- **We don't "pitch."** We assess whether we can help. A prospect who isn't the right fit is told so, directly. This builds more trust than any marketing copy can.
- **The founder story is the product.** 30 years on the ground in Asia + Mandarin/Taiwanese fluency + QC expertise is not a feature list — it's the thing being sold. Every sales conversation should route back to it.
- **Trust > speed.** The sales cycle for an importer choosing a sourcing partner is 30–90 days typical, because the buyer is being asked to place real money at risk with an overseas partner. Never accelerate past the buyer's comfort level.
- **The buyer's fear is the anchor.** Importers have been burned: wrong specs, bad batches, factories that ghost after deposit. Every sales touchpoint should implicitly address those three fears.

Compare this to a generic trading company sales motion ("we have X product, it costs Y, buy it") — that's a bazaar sale. We're selling a relationship with capability behind it.

## II. Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

### Primary ICP: The Mid-Market Importer With a Language/Distance Problem

**Firmographics:**
- Company size: 10–500 employees
- Revenue: $5M–$250M/year
- Location: US (primary), Canada, UK, EU, Australia, select Latin America
- Industry: Distributors, retailers, contractors, private-label brands, e-commerce retailers, hospitality procurement, building-materials wholesalers

**Product verticals (prioritized, matched to our sourcing strength):**
1. **Flooring** — wholesalers, flooring retailers, flooring contractors, builders with a flooring line. Lead vertical.
2. **Garments & fabrics** — apparel brands, private-label programs, home textiles retailers, uniform manufacturers. Second vertical.
3. **Bathroom products & ironmongery** — plumbing distributors, hardware distributors, hospitality/hotel procurement, kitchen-and-bath retailers.
4. **Travel accessories** — retailers, brands, e-commerce operators.

**Behavioral / trigger signals (most important):**
- Currently importing from Asia via a trading agent, broker, or directly via Alibaba with quality complaints
- Recently had a QC failure, shipment rejected at port, or customs issue
- Expanding into a new SKU line that requires Asian sourcing
- Diversifying out of mainland China (friend-shoring to Taiwan, Vietnam, etc.)
- Private-label or house-brand expansion
- Job change: new head of sourcing, VP supply chain, or CEO (90-day "new boss" window = highest buy-propensity)

**Titles to target:**
- Owner / CEO / President (for SMBs under ~50 employees — they make the sourcing call directly)
- VP/Director of Sourcing or Procurement
- Head of Supply Chain
- Category Buyer (for retailers)
- Product Manager (for private-label brands)
- COO (for operationally-driven SMBs)

### Secondary ICP: The Buyer Without a Sourcing Partner Yet

Companies who source domestically but are exploring Asian sourcing for the first time. Longer education cycle, higher trust requirement, but also lower competition — they don't have an incumbent agent to displace.

### Anti-ICP (Do Not Pursue)

- **One-off transactions under $10K FOB.** Our model doesn't work at that volume — our 5% commission won't cover the sourcing effort.
- **Companies on sanctions-exposed product categories we don't handle** (dual-use goods, military, etc.).
- **Prospects in sanctioned jurisdictions** — screen via compliance skill before first email, not after.
- **Prospects whose products require deep category expertise we don't have** (e.g., pharmaceutical APIs, aerospace-grade materials).
- **Companies asking for "cheapest possible price, no questions asked"** — they will optimize for bottom-feeder suppliers and we'll lose the deal to a corner-cutter; we should be OK with that.

### ICP Scoring (Use on Every Prospect Before Outreach)

Score each prospect 1–5 on these dimensions. Only pursue if total ≥ 18/25:

| Dimension | Score |
|---|---|
| Product fit (flooring/garments/bath/ironmongery/travel) | 1–5 |
| Volume fit ($100K+/year likely) | 1–5 |
| Trigger signal present (recent pain, expansion, new hire) | 1–5 |
| Decision-maker accessibility (title + seniority) | 1–5 |
| Geography fit (US/Canada/EU/UK/AU/selected LATAM) | 1–5 |

## III. The Outbound Sales Cadence

### Channel Mix

**Primary: Cold email** — lowest cost per touch, highest leverage for international outreach (no timezone friction), easiest to track and improve.

**Secondary: LinkedIn** — connection request + personalized message. Higher intimacy, slower, but converts well for senior titles (VP+).

**Tertiary: Phone** — only after email/LinkedIn engagement. Cold-calling a US procurement manager from a foreign number is low-yield and often blocked.

**Never: WhatsApp/WeChat cold.** Reserve those for relationship stages after email has earned the right.

### The 9-Touch Cadence (Email + LinkedIn)

Sovern House's default outbound sequence. Adjust based on vertical and signal strength.

**Day 0 — Email 1 (The Opener)**
- Short. 60–90 words. Plain text. No logo, no banner.
- Lead with a specific, researched observation about their business (trigger signal).
- One sentence on relevance — why you, why now.
- Soft CTA: "worth a 15-minute call?" or "should I send you a short note on how we work?"
- See `trade-outreach-copy.md` for full templates by vertical.

**Day 2 — LinkedIn connection request (no note, or a 2-line contextual note)**
- Accepts rate jumps ~40% vs. blind connect when there's a recent email context.

**Day 4 — Email 2 (The Value Add)**
- Do not resend or "bump to the top of your inbox." That's a spam signal.
- Instead, send something useful: a short market note, a link to a case study, a specific data point about their category.
- Example: "Saw this — Taiwan flooring exports up 14% YoY as US buyers diversify from China. If you're looking at Taiwan sourcing I have direct factory relationships there. Worth a chat?"

**Day 7 — LinkedIn InMail or direct message (if connection accepted)**
- One short message referencing the email.
- Mirror any post they've recently made — shows you're paying attention.

**Day 10 — Email 3 (The Reframe)**
- Acknowledge no reply without apology.
- Offer a different angle. If Email 1 focused on QC, Email 3 focuses on cost or lead time.
- "Different angle — the thing most buyers don't realise about Taiwan flooring is [specific insight]."

**Day 14 — Voice note or video via LinkedIn or email (optional, high-intimacy)**
- 30-second personalized video from Alex. Powerful for senior decision-makers but time-intensive. Reserve for top 10% of prospects.

**Day 18 — Email 4 (The Short Check-in)**
- 2 sentences.
- "Is this a priority this quarter or should I circle back next?"

**Day 25 — Email 5 (The Breakup)**
- Acknowledge the silence, move on gracefully, leave door open.
- "Closing the loop — I'll stop reaching out. If anything changes on [specific trigger], my door's open."
- Breakup emails often generate 3–7% reply rate. They work because they remove the implicit pressure.

**Day 45 — Handoff to quarterly nurture**
- This is a stage transition, not another touch. The prospect leaves the outbound cadence and enters the quarterly nurture list.
- Quarterly nurture = one genuinely useful piece of content per quarter (market note, tariff update, specific factory-level observation). Not a newsletter. Not "just checking in."
- Retain the prospect in CRM indefinitely with stage = "Nurture" until either: they re-engage (move back to active cadence), they explicitly unsubscribe (move to suppression list — permanent), or they signal a trigger event that justifies a fresh outbound sequence with a new angle.

### Cadence Rules

- **Never more than 1 touch per 48 hours.** Otherwise you read as desperate.
- **Stop immediately on any reply**, including "not interested" or "wrong person." Human reply = move to human handling, not auto-followup.
- **Sanctions screen every prospect before touch 1** (L-013 in lessons.md). OFAC SDN, EU Consolidated, UN. See `trade-compliance.md` and `trade-prospecting-data.md`.
- **Quality over quantity.** 30 prospects/day with deep personalization beats 300 generic sends. Deliverability data (2026): personalized emails reply-rate at 2× generic templates; heavily researched trigger-based emails hit 18%+ reply.
- **Verticalize the cadence.** A flooring wholesaler and a garment importer get different subject lines, different proof points, different case studies.

## IV. Qualification Framework — SOVERN-Q

BANT is too shallow for trade deals (volume matters more than budget; authority can be a single owner). MEDDIC is too SaaS-shaped. Here's a framework tuned for international trade buying-house deals:

**S — Scale**
How much volume are they actually talking about? Containers/year? FOB value/year? Under $100K total FOB/year is usually not viable for our commission model (unless it's a multi-year contract or high-margin category).

**O — Origin / Origin fit**
Are they sourcing from or planning to source from Asia (our strength)? Or are they asking us to source from somewhere we don't cover (Eastern Europe, Africa, Americas)? Origin-mismatch kills many deals fast; catch it early.

**V — Vertical fit**
Flooring, garments, bathroom, ironmongery, travel accessories, logs. Outside those? Probe for adjacency. A buyer asking about "Chinese kitchen cabinets" = bathroom/ironmongery adjacent = worth pursuing. A buyer asking about "Chinese electronic components" = not our strength, refer out.

**E — Economic buyer**
Who signs the cheque? In SMBs it's often the CEO/owner and you might already be talking to them. In mid-market it's usually VP of Sourcing with CFO sign-off above a threshold. Always identify the economic buyer by call 2, or the deal stalls.

**R — Risk / pain**
What's the pain that brought them to us? Quality failure? Tariff shock? Lost supplier? Capacity constraint? Deals close when pain is acute and recent. "Just exploring" = low priority.

**N — Next step**
Is there a concrete next step they've agreed to? Sample order? Factory audit? Site visit? If there's no next step with a date attached, it's not qualified yet.

**Q — Quick**
Realistic decision timeline. 30 days? 6 months? 18 months? Match cadence to timeline.

### Scoring (Use in CRM)

Each letter scored 0–3:
- **0** = deal-killer (fails qualification)
- **1** = concern (probe more before pursuing)
- **2** = healthy
- **3** = strong

Minimum qualified deal: every dimension ≥ 1, total ≥ 14/21.

### Red-flag answers

- "Budget's not set yet, we're exploring" + no trigger event → low priority, slow cadence
- "We need the cheapest possible price, period" → probe for quality expectations; if none, walk away (they'll chase a corner-cutter)
- "Can you give us a price now, over email, for these specs?" → signals a quote-shopper, not a partner buyer. Explain our model first; they'll self-select.
- Refusal to do a discovery call → not a partner-type buyer, might still be a principal-trade opportunity
- "Our current agent does XYZ" with no complaint → low displacement urgency; nurture slowly

## V. The Discovery Call

First real call with a qualified prospect. Goals:
1. Deepen qualification (SOVERN-Q)
2. Position the buying-house model explicitly (5% flat, no hidden markup) — this is where many deals convert because competitors won't say it out loud
3. Surface the pain in detail — what went wrong last time, what they're afraid of
4. Get explicit agreement on next step with date

### Structure (30 min default)

- **0–2 min — Frame.** "This call is for me to understand your business well enough to know if we can actually help. If we can't, I'll tell you. If we can, I'll walk you through how we work."
- **2–15 min — Discovery.**
  - "Walk me through what you currently source from Asia."
  - "What do you wish was different about how you source today?"
  - "Tell me about the last time sourcing went wrong."
  - "Who else is involved in decisions like this?" (identify economic buyer + influencers)
  - "What's driving you to look at this now?" (trigger event)
  - "If we did a trial sourcing project, what product and what volume would make sense?"
- **15–25 min — Position.**
  - Explain the buying-house model. Walk through: sourcing → QC → docs → ship.
  - Explain the 5% commission structure with FOB transparency.
  - Give one relevant proof point from the founder's 30 years (don't perform — just the one most relevant example).
- **25–30 min — Close.**
  - "Does this sound like it could fit your next sourcing project?"
  - If yes: agree on a specific next step with a date. Trial sourcing quote? Factory pre-qual list? Reference call?
  - If no: ask what would need to change. Leave the door open with specificity.

### Discovery questions bank

Organized by theme. Use as needed; don't machine-gun them.

**On current state:**
- What percentage of your COGS is Asia-sourced today?
- Who owns your supplier relationships internally?
- What's your typical container-level order size?
- What Incoterms do you currently buy under? Why that choice?

**On pain:**
- When was the last time a shipment didn't meet spec? What happened?
- How do you handle QC today — in-house, third-party, trust the factory?
- What does a supplier switch cost you, end-to-end, including tooling and sample runs?
- What would a 10% landed-cost reduction mean for your business?

**On process:**
- How do you currently find new suppliers?
- How do you qualify them?
- What's your payment terms norm — LC, TT, open account?
- Who signs off on a new supplier relationship?

**On trust & fit:**
- Have you worked with a buying agent before? What did/didn't work?
- What would make you feel confident choosing a partner in Asia?
- What reference calls would you want to do before placing a real order?

## VI. Pipeline Stages

Simple, 6-stage pipeline. Keep it clean — complex pipelines break first.

| Stage | Definition | Exit criteria to move forward |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Prospect** | In target list, not yet touched | Sanctions-screened; first email sent |
| **2. Engaged** | Has opened, clicked, replied, or accepted LinkedIn | Discovery call booked OR one substantive reply |
| **3. Qualifying** | Discovery call done; SOVERN-Q in progress | SOVERN-Q ≥ 14/21 with named economic buyer |
| **4. Proposing** | Working on scoped quote, trial sourcing plan, or factory shortlist | Proposal delivered; pricing terms agreed in principle |
| **5. Closing** | Negotiating contract / PO / sample run | Signed MSA OR first PO issued |
| **6. Active** | First deal done; ongoing commercial relationship | n/a — this is the goal state |

### Stage health checks

- Any prospect in Stage 2 for more than 21 days = stale. Move to nurture or kill.
- Any prospect in Stage 3 for more than 45 days without a named economic buyer = stall. Either name-escalate or deprioritize.
- Any prospect in Stage 4 for more than 60 days without a signed PO = red flag. Diagnose: are they a real buyer or a quote-shopper?
- Every prospect needs a next step with a date. No date = no stage.

### Loss reasons (track these)

Track every lost deal with a reason code. Patterns tell you what's breaking:
- Price (lost to lower-cost agent)
- Trust (couldn't overcome "but how do I know you'll deliver")
- Timing (not ready to buy)
- Fit (wrong vertical or volume)
- Competitor (named incumbent won)
- Internal (buyer lost budget, restructure, etc.)
- No decision (ghosted / didn't prioritize)

## VII. Reply Handling

### Positive intent replies

**"Sure, send me more info."**
- Do NOT send a brochure. Send a 2-paragraph email restating their specific situation + one case study link + calendar link.
- Book the call within 72 hours; intent decays fast.

**"What do you charge?"**
- Answer directly: "Flat 5% of FOB for standard sourcing. Smaller orders or private-label programs run higher (typically 7–10%) because of the fixed sourcing cost. Happy to walk you through exactly what's included in a quick call."
- Never evade this question. Evasion signals hidden markup.

**"Send me a sample."**
- Probe first: specs? volume target? timeline? budget expectation? The sample is the first real touchpoint — don't ship blind.
- Samples below $200 FOB: send free, recover in deal.
- Samples above $200 FOB: charge + credit against first order.

### Skeptical replies

**"How do I know you're real?"**
- Celebrate this question. Offer: reference calls, Taiwan office video call, factory video tour, LinkedIn + industry verification, Taiwan business registration number, Alex's Mandarin/Taiwanese language test on a live call.
- Never get defensive. "Good question, here's how we address that" + specifics.

**"Why should I switch from my current agent?"**
- Don't attack the incumbent. Ask: "Tell me what's working well, and what you wish was better." The buyer will tell you where to position.
- Offer a parallel trial — run one SKU through us while keeping your incumbent. Low switching risk.

**"Your prices seem too good / too high."**
- Too good: explain the economics. "Our 5% is the full cost. Agents who quote 3% often have hidden spread at the factory."
- Too high: ask what they're comparing to. Often they're comparing to no-agent (Alibaba direct) — in which case the pitch is what goes wrong without a partner.

### Rejection replies

**"Not interested" / "We're all set"**
- One-line acknowledgement. Move to quarterly nurture.
- "Understood. I'll close the loop. If anything changes — particularly around [specific scenario] — my door's open."

**"Wrong person, try X"**
- Thank them. Mention their name in the first line to the referral. Warm intros close 5× better than cold.

**No reply after full cadence**
- Not dead, just dormant. Move to quarterly nurture. Don't restart the full cadence — send one useful piece of content per quarter.

## VIII. Negotiating — Principles for Trade Deals

### Non-negotiable (protect these)

- **The 5% flat structure.** It's the positioning moat. Discounting below 5% unravels the buying-house story and trains the buyer to negotiate on fee in every deal. If you need to concede, concede on commercial terms (volume tier, payment schedule, inspection scope) before touching the commission rate.
- **Sanctions/compliance screening.** Not a negotiable item; we screen every counterparty every time.
- **QC step.** A buyer who wants to skip QC to save fee is buying trouble. Our position: we won't skip QC because our name is on the shipment. If they want QC-free they're not our buyer.
- **Written documentation.** Every agreed term in writing. No "we'll figure it out later."

### Negotiable (structure levers that protect the moat)

- Commission tiers for volume (5% at first tier, 4% at higher tier, etc. — but only with guaranteed volumes, not speculative ones)
- Payment schedule (30/70, 10/90, LC, TT — pick what reduces their risk without adding to ours)
- QC scope (pre-production sample + pre-shipment inspection is standard; add midline inspection for high-risk)
- Trial sizing (first order at reduced scope to build trust)
- Reporting cadence (weekly updates, shared dashboard, milestone checklist)

### Deal-killers to spot

- Buyer insists on paying agent fee at their preferred "market rate" but wants full service — walk.
- Buyer wants us to buy-and-resell (principal trade) but won't accept transparent spread — consider carefully; principal is a different product with different risk.
- Buyer's payment terms require us to extend credit beyond 30 days on a first deal — no; LC or TT on first deal. Earn longer terms later.
- Buyer wants exclusive territory without volume commitment — no; exclusivity without volume is a free option.

### Incoterms discipline (per `trade-operations.md`)

Never quote without Incoterm specified. A "$2.50/sqm" flooring quote is meaningless without knowing FOB vs. CIF vs. DDP. Every quote includes Incoterm, port/destination, and currency.

## IX. CRM Hygiene

Every prospect record must have:
- Company name, legal entity, country, website
- Sanctions screening status + date (critical — L-013)
- Economic buyer named (once qualified)
- SOVERN-Q score
- Stage + date entered stage
- Next step + date
- Last touch + channel
- Source (where did this lead come from)
- Loss reason if lost

Weekly hygiene discipline:
- Any record without next-step-date = stale; fix or archive
- Any "Active" record without an order in 90 days = revisit relationship
- Any "Qualifying" record > 45 days = diagnose stall

### Recommended tools by stage

**Pre-revenue / <$1M ARR of sourcing commissions:**
- HubSpot Free CRM — $0. Contacts, deals, basic pipeline. Pair with a sales-sequence tool (Instantly or Smartlead) for cadences.

**$1M–$5M ARR:**
- HubSpot Starter ($15/seat/mo) or Pipedrive ($14-$29/seat/mo). Adds automation.

**$5M+:**
- Consider the ERP's CRM module directly if it's been extended for pipeline use. Reason: single-source-of-truth across sourcing, QC, shipping, and sales. Our own ERP is purpose-built for trade operations; most CRMs aren't.

## XI. What the Head of Sales Blocks

**Never approve:**
1. Sending outbound to a prospect not sanctions-screened (L-013)
2. Discounting the 5% commission as the first negotiation lever (unravels positioning)
3. Sending a quote without Incoterm, origin, currency, and lead time specified
4. Extending credit terms (open account) on a first deal with a new buyer
5. Taking a deal outside our sourcing verticals without explicit leadership sign-off
6. Sending cold emails from sovernhouse.co primary domain (must route via secondary sending domain — see `trade-email-deliverability.md`)
7. Taking a buyer call without having researched their business first — 10 minutes of LinkedIn + website + Panjiva/ImportGenius before every call
8. Closing a deal without written terms
9. Promising capability we don't have ("Yes we can source aerospace-grade titanium") to get a foot in the door
10. Re-engaging a prospect who said "stop" — that's a one-and-done, not a slow nurture opportunity

**Flag for review before proceeding:**
- Any deal structured as principal trade rather than brokerage (different risk profile)
- Any multi-year exclusive arrangement
- Any deal requiring us to carry inventory
- Any buyer asking us to coordinate non-Asia sourcing
- Any deal with a buyer flagged by sanctions screening at any tier (direct, beneficial owner, vessels)
