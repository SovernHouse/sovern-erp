## I. Brand Voice & Positioning

### Core Brand Pillars

**Credibility Through Evidence**
- International trade demands proof. Every claim must be backed by third-party validation, case studies, certifications (ISO, export compliance, industry-specific), or verifiable track record.
- Never make promises you can't substantiate. A broken promise on a trade website can mean a lost deal and damaged reputation across suppliers and buyers in your market.

**Reliability as a Differentiator**
- For international buyers and suppliers, reliability is trust. You must demonstrate consistent execution: timely responses, accurate quotes, predictable delivery, compliance with documentation requirements.
- Messaging: "30-year track record in [commodity/sector]," "100% on-time delivery rate," "ISO 9001 & [sector-specific certifications]"

**Expertise, Not Just Inventory**
- Position as an advisor, not just a transaction facilitator. International trade buyers and suppliers choose partners who understand their market, logistics, tariffs, and risk.
- Show domain knowledge: market insights, regulatory awareness, logistics optimization, currency/payment strategy.

**Accessibility in Complexity**
- International trade is complex (Incoterms, HS codes, sanctions lists, letters of credit, bills of lading). Your positioning should make this complexity feel navigable.
- Messaging: "From sourcing to delivery, we handle the complexity," "Your single point of contact for [region] trade"

### Tone & Voice

- **Professional, not stiff.** Clear and direct. Avoid jargon unless your audience uses it; when you do, define it.
- **Honest about limitations.** If you don't service a region, don't pretend. If a deal doesn't fit your model, say so. This builds trust faster than overcommitting.
- **Human, not corporate.** Buyers and suppliers are making bets. Let them see the people behind the company—team photos, LinkedIn profiles, email signatures with real names.
- **Respect for cultural context.** Avoid idioms, sports metaphors, or culturally specific humor. These don't translate and can offend.
- **Transparent about process.** Explain how you operate: your lead time, how you source, your quality checks, your compliance protocols. Demystify the process.

### Brand Positioning Statement (Internal Reference)

*[Company Name] is the trusted trade partner for [target sectors: commodities/manufactured goods/services] across [target geographies]. We differentiate through deep market expertise, 100% regulatory compliance, transparent operations, and a commitment to long-term partnerships. We are the advisor international buyers and suppliers choose when complexity, risk, and scale matter.*

## II. Marketing Priorities (Ranked by Impact)

### Tier 1: Non-Negotiable (Do These First)

**1. Website as Credibility Infrastructure**
- Your website is your 24/7 first impression. It must communicate: who you are, what you trade, which geographies you serve, certifications/compliance, how to contact you, and how to request a quote.
- Benchmark: 70% of B2B websites fail basic AI search tests. Yours must not.
- Tactical: Clear product pages with specs, high-quality photography (real operations, not stock), ISO certificates with verification links, team bios, client logos (with permission), case study links.

**2. SEO for International Markets**
- If you're not findable in target markets' search results (Google, local equivalents), you don't exist to them.
- Localized content (not translated content) performs 70% better. Each target market needs keyword research, messaging, and content in local language and terminology.
- Benchmark: Implement geo-targeting, hreflang tags, and country-specific content by Q2 2026.

**3. Trust Signals & Verification**
- Display certifications prominently with verification links (note: certificate numbers and issuing body).
- Client testimonials (even if anonymous, they must be specific: "Reduced lead time by 40%" beats "Great to work with").
- Case studies with measurable outcomes: "Sourced 500 tonnes of [commodity] at 8% below market rate, delivered 3 weeks ahead of schedule."
- Response time guarantee: "Respond to all RFQs within 24 hours" (then deliver on it).

### Tier 2: High-Impact (3–6 Months)

**4. LinkedIn & Thought Leadership**
- LinkedIn is where international buyers and suppliers research suppliers. You must be visible, credible, and active.
- Post 2–5 times/week (consistency > frequency). Carousel posts get 11.2x more impressions than text-only.
- Content: market insights, regulatory updates, logistics tips, case studies, industry trends.
- Personal profiles: Ensure your team (esp. CEO/business development) posts company insights. Personal posts convert better than corporate-only pages.

**5. Gated Content & Webinars**
- High-quality leads generated through gated content convert 36% more often to sales-qualified leads than other sources.
- Webinar cost-per-lead is $72, vs. $800+ for trade shows. On-demand webinars capture 47% of views.
- Content ideas: "Navigating [region] tariffs 2026," "Letters of credit 101," "How to structure [commodity] contracts," "Currency hedging for importers."

**6. Trade Show & Exhibition Strategy**
- 70% of exhibitors fail to follow up after shows. You will not be in this group.
- Hot leads: Follow up within 24–48 hours. Use CRM to track, personalize, and ensure timely outreach.
- Pre-show: Drive registration with targeted emails and social ads.
- Post-show: Launch follow-up campaign to all collected leads (email sequence, personal calls, secondary mailings).

### Tier 3: Scaling (6–12 Months)

**7. Email Nurture & Marketing Automation**
- Design sequences for RFQ follow-up, cold prospects, and post-webinar nurture.
- Trigger-based: When someone downloads a guide, views 3+ product pages, or attends a webinar, activate the next sequence.

**8. Industry Partnerships & Distribution**
- Partner with local distributors in target markets (76% of successful global B2B companies do this).
- Co-brand content: Partner with logistics providers, banks, industry associations to co-publish guides.

**9. Paid Advertising (LinkedIn, Google, Search)**
- Only after organic channels are optimized. B2B trade buyers are high-intent but low-volume. Target by job title, company size, industry.
- Start with LinkedIn Ads for thought leadership content; Google Ads for high-intent keywords (e.g., "buy [commodity] [region]").

## III. Content Strategy

### Content Types That Drive Trade Leads

**Educational Content (High Priority)**
- Guides & how-tos: "Your First Import: Step-by-Step," "Understanding Incoterms," "Customs Documentation Checklist," "How to Verify Supplier Credentials"
- Explainers: Tariff updates, trade agreements, regulatory changes by region
- Frequency: 1 guide per month; 1 regulatory update per quarter per region

**Case Studies (Critical)**
- Real deals with (anonymized if needed) measurable outcomes: volume sourced, price achieved, timeline, quality improvements
- Structure: Problem, approach, result, time saved/cost saved
- Target: 1 case study per major sector/geography per quarter
- Distribution: Website, LinkedIn, email, gated content, sales collateral

**Market Reports & Trend Analysis (Thought Leadership)**
- Quarterly market snapshots: "Q1 2026 Commodity Price Trends in SE Asia," "EU Trade Regulation Changes & What They Mean for Importers"
- These position you as an expert and attract backlinks and referrals
- Frequency: 1 full report per quarter; 2–3 shorter trend pieces per month

**Product/Specification Sheets (Conversion-Focused)**
- High-quality specs with photography, certifications, sourcing regions, lead times, minimum orders, pricing structure
- Each should link directly to RFQ form
- Update quarterly as pricing/availability changes

**Testimonials & Logos**
- Collect specific testimonials from clients (with permission): name, company, role, quote about your service
- Logo placement on website builds credibility instantly
- Even 3–5 well-placed logos outperform generic testimonials

**Email Content (Nurture)**
- Educational: 50% (market tips, regulatory updates, guides)
- Promotional: 30% (case studies, special offerings, webinar invitations)
- Relationship: 20% (company updates, team spotlights, industry news)
- Send 1 email per week to engaged segments; monthly to cold lists

### Content Calendar Structure

| Quarter | Primary Focus | Deliverables |
|---------|---------------|----|
| Q1 | Region/sector deep-dive | 1 market report, 1 case study, 2 guides, 4 blog posts |
| Q2 | Regulatory/compliance | 1 compliance guide, 1 webinar, 1 case study, 4 blog posts |
| Q3 | Logistics & operations | 1 logistics guide, 1 case study, 1 webinar, 4 blog posts |
| Q4 | Year-end trends & planning | 1 annual market outlook, 1–2 case studies, 4 blog posts |

**Distribution schedule per asset:**
- Publish on website
- Email to list (with teaser)
- LinkedIn post + share across team profiles
- Trade publication outreach (for larger pieces)
- Gated version for lead generation (if applicable)

## IV. SEO Strategy

### Keyword Categories (Research & Prioritize These)

**Buyer-Intent Keywords (Highest Priority)**
- "Buy [commodity] [country]" (e.g., "buy cocoa beans Ecuador")
- "Sourcing [product] [region]"
- "Import [commodity] to [destination]"
- "[Product] supplier [country]"
- "[Commodity] price [region]"

**Supplier-Intent Keywords**
- "Export [product] [region]"
- "Sell [commodity] internationally"
- "[Product] trading company"

**Educational Keywords (Awareness)**
- "How to import [commodity]"
- "Incoterms explained"
- "Tariffs [country] [product]"
- "Letter of credit [region]"
- "[Commodity] market trends 2026"

**Competitor Keywords**
- Monitor competitor sites and their top-ranking pages. Find gaps you can own.

### Technical SEO for International Markets

**Geo-Targeting & hreflang**
- Use hreflang tags to tell Google which version of your site to show to users in different regions
- Example: en-US, en-GB, es-ES, zh-CN, pt-BR
- Each region gets its own localized content, not just translated

**Site Structure**
- Option A: Subdomains (es.yoursite.com, br.yoursite.com) — stronger regional signals
- Option B: Subfolders (/es/, /br/) — easier to manage, slightly weaker signals
- Choose based on your target markets and resources

**Content Localization, Not Translation**
- Hire native speakers or local SEO experts to research keywords and adapt messaging for each market
- Currency, units of measure, terminology, industry standards vary by region
- Example: HS codes, Incoterms usage, payment terms preferences differ by market

**Technical Basics**
- Mobile-responsive design (mandatory; 80% of B2B research happens on mobile)
- Page speed: Aim for <3 seconds globally (use CDN, compress images, lazy-load)
- Structured data (Schema.org): Mark up product details, certifications, company info
- XML sitemaps for each language/region

### Link Building Strategy

- Pursue links from trade publications, industry associations, and local directories in each target market
- Partner with complementary services (logistics, finance, customs brokers) for co-marketing and mutual linking
- Create linkable assets: original research, industry reports, interactive tools (tariff calculators, supplier verification guides)

### Content Visibility

- 30–50 pieces of content must be consumed before a B2B buyer makes a decision
- You need visibility across search, not just top-3 rankings
- Repurpose content: 1 case study becomes a blog post, LinkedIn post, email series, social graphic, video snippet

## V. Channel Strategy (Prioritized with Rationale)

### Tier 1: Always-On Channels (Non-Negotiable)

**Website & SEO (Owned)**
- Your owned asset. Every other channel drives here. Invest first.
- ROI: High (organic traffic is free after initial investment). Compound over time.
- Effort: High upfront; moderate ongoing.

**LinkedIn (Organic + Paid)**
- B2B buyers and suppliers actively use LinkedIn for research and sourcing
- Owned reach on company page + paid ads for high-intent targeting
- ROI: Medium (lower cost than traditional channels; slower than some digital tactics)
- Effort: Moderate (2–5 posts/week, response management, paid campaign setup)

**Email Marketing (Owned)**
- Highest ROI of all channels for B2B (typically 40:1, meaning every $1 spent returns $40)
- You own the list; not dependent on algorithm changes
- ROI: Very high
- Effort: Moderate (once list is built; regular content needed)

### Tier 2: High-Impact Channels (6-Month Build)

**Trade Shows & Exhibitions (Earned/Paid)**
- Where your buyers and suppliers physically gather. Industry-specific = high-intent audience
- Cost per lead: $800+, but attendees are pre-qualified
- Strategy: Select 2–3 shows per year in key markets; heavy pre-show promotion; 24–48-hour follow-up on all leads
- Effort: High (pre-show, booth management, post-show follow-up)
- Rationale: International partnerships built at shows. Virtual shows don't replace physical presence entirely.

**Webinars & Virtual Events (Owned/Paid)**
- Cost per lead: $72 (much cheaper than shows)
- 73% of B2B marketers say webinars generate highest-quality leads
- On-demand webinars: 47% of views come after initial broadcast; evergreen asset
- Frequency: 1 webinar per quarter minimum
- Rationale: Low barrier to attendance; captures international audience; builds authority

**Industry Publications & Trade Magazines (Earned/Paid)**
- Articles in relevant trade publications signal authority and reach buyers in their trusted editorial environment
- Paid sponsorships or contributed articles
- Effort: Medium
- ROI: Medium (brand building + backlinks; harder to track direct leads)
- Rationale: International buyers read trade publications in their industry and region. Your article = third-party credibility

### Tier 3: Scaling Channels (Invest When Tier 1 & 2 Stabilized)

**Paid Advertising (Google Ads, LinkedIn Ads, Retargeting)**
- Only execute once organic channels prove keywords and messaging work
- Start with LinkedIn Ads targeting by job title, company size, industry
- Google Ads for high-intent keywords (buyer-intent keywords identified in SEO section)
- Retargeting: Visitors who viewed product pages or downloaded guides but didn't convert
- Effort: Moderate (ongoing optimization, A/B testing)
- Rationale: Scale fast-moving, high-intent opportunities; but not cheaper than earned/organic over time

**Partnerships & Co-Marketing (Earned)**
- Partner with complementary services: logistics providers, banks, customs brokers, industry associations
- Co-publish guides, webinars, case studies
- Joint email campaigns to each other's lists
- Effort: Moderate
- Rationale: Extends reach into new audiences; signals credibility through association

**Industry Directories & B2B Marketplaces (Owned/Earned)**
- Alibaba, Global Sources, TradeKey, EC21, local equivalents in your target markets
- Profile optimization: complete information, certifications, company photos, reviews
- Costs vary (some free; premium listings $100–1000/year)
- Effort: Low to moderate (setup and monitoring)
- Rationale: Buyers use these to find suppliers; your presence is table stakes in some sectors

**Thought Leadership & PR (Earned)**
- Seek speaking opportunities at industry conferences
- Pitch company insights to business press and trade publications
- Publish original research reports
- Effort: Medium
- ROI: Slower to measure; long-term brand and authority building

### Channels That Don't Work for International Trade (Avoid)

- TikTok (wrong audience for B2B trade)
- Influencer marketing (irrelevant to professional sourcing decisions)
- Generic display ads (low intent, poor conversion)
- Facebook (much lower B2B engagement than LinkedIn)
- Cold calling without prior contact (low ROI for trade; LinkedIn outreach more effective)

## VI. Lead Generation Playbook

### RFQ (Request for Quote) Form — Core Lead Generation Tool

**Design Principles**
- 3–5 fields maximum on first screen: Company name, Contact name, Email, Product needed, Quantity/timeline
- "Additional Details" can be optional or expandable
- Clear value prop above form: "Get a custom quote within 24 hours"
- Mobile-optimized: Form must work on mobile (50%+ of traffic)
- Immediate confirmation: "We've received your RFQ. You'll hear from us within 24 hours."

**Placement Strategy**
- Every product page links directly to RFQ form
- Website header/navigation has clear "Request a Quote" button
- Landing pages for paid campaigns link to RFQ form
- Email campaigns include RFQ CTA

**Follow-Up Process (Critical)**
- 1st touch: Within 1 hour (automated thank-you email with RFQ reference number)
- 2nd touch: Within 24 hours (personal email from sales/business development with preliminary quote or next steps)
- 3rd touch: Within 72 hours (phone call or video call if not yet responded)
- Track all touches in CRM; never let an RFQ go unanswered

### Gated Content (Lead Magnets)

**High-Converting Assets**
- Guides: "Navigating [Region] Trade Regulations," "Your Supplier Verification Checklist"
- Templates: Contract templates, RFQ templates, packing list examples
- Market reports: Regional commodity price trends, tariff forecasts
- Tools: Incoterm calculator, landed cost calculator, tariff lookup tool

**Gate Formula**
- Low friction: Ask for company name, email, role (3 fields max)
- High value: Asset must be genuinely useful, not a sales pitch
- Immediate delivery: Email the asset within 5 minutes of signup
- Follow-up: Nurture sequence activates after signup (educational emails, not hard sell)

**Measurement**
- Track: Conversion rate (% of page visitors who gate), downstream behavior (% who open nurture emails, download case study, request quote)
- Target: 25%+ conversion rate on landing page

### Webinars (Quarterly Minimum)

**Topic Formula**
- Solve a pain point international traders face: tariffs, documentation, logistics, risk mitigation, compliance
- Examples: "Smart Currency Hedging for Importers," "5 Customs Violations That Cost Million-Dollar Deals (And How to Avoid Them)," "Finding Reliable Suppliers in [Region]"

**Promotion**
- 3 weeks before: Email existing list, LinkedIn ads, organic posts
- 1 week before: Reminder sequence via email
- After registration: Pre-webinar emails with agenda and speaker bios

**During Webinar**
- 30 minutes max (Q&A included)
- Slides designed for readability (large text, not too much data)
- Offer companion guide or checklist to download (gated) after the live broadcast

**After Webinar**
- Send recording to registrants immediately
- Publish on-demand version on website (gated, for lead capture)
- Share highlights on LinkedIn, email
- 48-hour follow-up: "Didn't get to your Q&A? Reply to this email"

**Measurement**
- Registrations, attendance rate, Q&A engagement, gated asset downloads, downstream conversions (% who download case study, request quote)

### Cold Outreach via LinkedIn (Low Priority, But Valid)

- Search for decision-makers by industry, company size, region
- Personalized connection request: "Hi [Name], I noticed [Company] is expanding into [Region]. We just sourced [commodity] for similar operations. Worth a 15-min call?"
- Don't send sales pitch in first message. Goal: permission to talk
- Follow up after 5 days if no response (once); then archive
- Measure: Response rate, meeting conversion rate

### Trade Show Follow-Up (24–48 Hours, Non-Negotiable)

- Badge scans or business card data must be entered into CRM same day as show
- Organize by "hot" (engaged for 10+ min, asked specific questions) vs. "warm" (interested but not urgent)
- Hot leads: Personal email within 24 hours + phone call within 48 hours
- Warm leads: Email sequence starting within 72 hours
- Template for email: Reference specific conversation ("You asked about [product] availability in [region]"), offer next step (call, sample, pricing), include CTA button

## VII. Cultural Considerations for International Marketing

### Language & Localization (Non-Negotiable)

**Translation ≠ Localization**
- Do not use Google Translate or automated tools for customer-facing content
- Hire native speakers for website, emails, and key assets
- Test with local partners before launch (idioms, terminology, tone don't always translate)

**Examples of Cultural Pitfalls to Avoid**
- Sports metaphors ("hit it out of the park," "ballpark figure") — don't translate
- Idioms ("piece of cake," "low-hanging fruit") — nonsensical in other languages
- Colors: Red = luck in China, but danger in Germany; white = purity in West, mourning in East
- Numbers: 8 is lucky in China; 13 is unlucky in West; 4 sounds like "death" in Japan/China
- Use of "you" language: Some cultures prefer formal address; others prefer casual

**Messaging Adaptation by Region**

| Region | Key Drivers | Messaging Angle | Avoid |
|--------|-------------|---|---|
| **North America** | Speed, reliability, ROI | "Time-to-market," efficiency, compliance | Corporate jargon, vague promises |
| **Europe** | Quality, compliance, transparency | Certifications, environmental/ethical sourcing, transparency | Hype, exaggeration |
| **SE Asia** | Relationship, long-term partnership, flexibility | "Trusted partner," flexibility, personal touch | Hard sell, pressure tactics |
| **Latin America** | Personal relationships, cultural fit, reliability | Team bios, case studies with Spanish/Portuguese names, long-term vision | Impersonal processes, no account manager |
| **Middle East/Africa** | Trust, established reputation, regulatory compliance | Certifications, regulatory expertise, compliance | Assumptions about payment terms, bribes |

### Regional Preferences in Communication

- **Direct (US, Northern Europe):** Say what you mean. Be concise.
- **Formal (Germany, France):** Use proper titles and formal language.
- **Relationship-first (China, Latin America, Africa):** Invest in relationship before discussing business. Don't rush.
- **Indirect (Japan, SE Asia):** Suggest rather than demand. Allow face-saving options.

### Visual & Design Considerations

- Imagery: Include diverse team photos. Avoid stereotypes (e.g., don't use generic "happy factory workers" images in your manufacturing photos).
- Currency & Units: Display local currency conversions. Show both metric and imperial units where relevant.
- Testimonials: Include names and company logos from each major region.
- Legal & Compliance: Include local privacy policies, terms, and regulatory notices for each market.

### Payment Terms & Trust Signals by Region

- **US/Canada:** Credit card, Net 30–60
- **Europe:** SEPA transfer, Net 30, LC for larger deals
- **China/SE Asia:** Advance payment or letter of credit common
- **Latin America:** Trust-building leads to Net 30; advance payment for new suppliers
- **Middle East:** LC standard; relationship history enables longer terms

Make sure payment options and terms are transparent on your website for each market served.

## VIII. Copy Frameworks & Messaging Guidelines

### Homepage Copy Formula

**Headline:** "Your trusted [commodity/service] partner for [regions]"

**Subheading:** "Reliable sourcing, full compliance, transparent operations—from quote to delivery"

**Section 1:** "What we do" — bullet list of trading categories (commodities, manufactured goods, services, consulting). Example: "Cocoa, coffee, and specialty agricultural products from African suppliers; manufactured electronics components from SE Asia; customs and logistics consulting"

**Section 2:** "Why we're different"
- ISO 9001 certified and [relevant trade certifications]
- 100% on-time delivery record for [# of years]
- Team with [# of] years cumulative international trade experience
- Dedicated account managers, not ticket systems

**Section 3:** "How we work"
- 1) RFQ submission
- 2) 24-hour response with preliminary quote
- 3) Customized sourcing & verification
- 4) Transparent pricing and terms
- 5) Full documentation and compliance handling
- 6) Delivery & after-sales support

**CTA:** "Request a Quote" or "Schedule a 15-minute call"

### Product Page Copy Formula

**Title:** "[Commodity] from [Region]"

**Overview Paragraph:** What it is, primary use cases, and your advantage (quality grade, sourcing proximity, compliance, price point)

**Specifications Section:**
- Grade/quality standards
- Origin/sourcing
- Lead time
- Minimum order quantity
- Packaging and shipping options
- HS code and tariff classification

**Sourcing & Compliance:**
- How you verify suppliers
- Certifications you ensure
- Documentation provided (bills of lading, commercial invoices, certificates of origin, quality reports)

**Case Study or Testimonial:** Real example of how customers used this product; quantified results

**CTA:** "Request a quote for [Product]" or "Download spec sheet" (gated)

### Email Subject Lines (High-Intent, Direct)

- "Cocoa sourcing from Ghana — new harvest available"
- "[Commodity] price update — April 2026 trends"
- "Your RFQ #12345 — quote attached"
- "Webinar: 5 tariff changes affecting your imports"
- "Case study: How [Company] reduced cocoa costs by 12%"

**Avoid:**
- Click-bait ("You won't believe this trade secret...")
- Generic subject lines ("Greetings from [Company]")
- Too long (mobile truncates at ~50 characters)

### Case Study Template

**Headline:** "[Company] reduces [commodity] costs by X% while meeting compliance in [region]"

**Challenge Paragraph:** What was the problem? Price, availability, quality, compliance, lead time?

**Solution Paragraph:** How you approached it. Your process, relationships, expertise applied.

**Results Paragraph:** Quantified outcomes. Cost savings, timeline improvement, risk reduction, new market access. Specific numbers.

**Quote:** "This partnership transformed our [metric]. Working with [Company] gave us both pricing and peace of mind." — [Contact Name], [Title], [Company]

**Length:** 400–600 words; 1–2 images (real operations, not stock photos)

## IX. Items the CMO Blocks

**Never Approve:**
1. Unverified certifications or claims without proof ("ISO certified" without a certificate link and issuing body verification)
2. Testimonials without attribution (anonymous quotes undermine credibility in B2B trade)
3. Competitor bashing or negative commentary (damages your credibility; focus on your strengths)
4. Promises you can't guarantee (e.g., "fastest turnaround in the industry" without data)
5. Use of stock photos for operational imagery (buyers want to see real operations; stock photos signal you're hiding something)
6. Pricing without clear terms (price per unit, minimum orders, payment terms, delivery incoterm all must be explicit)
7. Content that assumes buyer location (e.g., "ship in 2 weeks" when that varies by region—specify shipping method and lead time by destination)
8. Testimonials from employees or internal stakeholders (only customer testimonials count)
9. CTA buttons that don't specify the next step (not "Contact us" but "Request a quote" or "Schedule a call")
10. Multilingual content done via automated translation without local review
11. Marketing campaigns that ignore local holidays, working hours, or customs (e.g., campaign launch on Chinese New Year when no one's working)
12. Any claim about tariffs, compliance, or regulations without citing the source (link to government regulatory body or established industry guide)
13. Product pages without clear HS codes and tariff info (international buyers need this; lack of it signals you're not serious about trade)

**Content That Requires Legal & Compliance Review Before Launch:**
- Any claims about product certifications (ISO, FSSC, HALAL, organic, etc.)
- Pricing guarantees or price-matching claims
- Statements about regulatory compliance or export/import legality
- Testimonials and case studies with specific measurable claims
- Any "first in [region]" or "only supplier of" claims

| Sales cycle length (lead to close) | Track by source; optimize longest | Quarterly | Identify which channels bring fastest-closing leads |
| Lead source attribution | Which channels drive most SQL-to-customer conversions? | Quarterly | Guides budget allocation |

## XI. Quarterly Review Checklist

**Every Quarter:**
- [ ] Review website traffic, RFQ submissions, and lead quality metrics
- [ ] Analyze which content pieces drove conversions; plan next quarter's topics accordingly
- [ ] Audit SEO rankings for target keywords; identify content gaps and competitor opportunities
- [ ] Review email performance; iterate on subject lines and CTAs
- [ ] LinkedIn: Review post performance; double down on high-engagement formats (carousels, case studies)
- [ ] Trade shows/events: Confirm follow-up completion; measure conversion rates
- [ ] Competitor scan: What content are they publishing? Where are gaps in your market coverage?
- [ ] Team feedback: Ask sales and customer service which questions/objections are most common; turn into content topics
- [ ] List health: Segment by engagement; remove inactive; nurture warm leads
- [ ] Budget allocation: Shift spend to highest-ROI channels; pause underperforming initiatives

**Semi-Annually (Every 6 Months):**
- [ ] Comprehensive SEO audit (rankings, backlinks, content gaps, technical health)
- [ ] Brand positioning review: Is messaging still resonating? Any competitive shifts?
- [ ] Webinar ROI analysis: Which topics drove most SQL conversions? Schedule next round
- [ ] Regional performance review: Which markets are responding? Where are we weak?
- [ ] CRM audit: Are we tracking leads correctly? Are follow-ups happening on time?

**Annually:**
- [ ] Full marketing plan review and next-year strategy
- [ ] Customer acquisition cost (CAC) and lifetime value (LTV) analysis
- [ ] Pricing and offer strategy: Are we positioning correctly against margin economics?
- [ ] Team capability review: What skills/tools do we need to invest in?
