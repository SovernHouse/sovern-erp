# CEO Decision Framework — International Trading Company

**Last Updated:** April 2026  
**For:** Leadership advising on trading company strategy and operations

---

## One-Sentence Business Model

Buy commodities, manufactured goods, or services from suppliers in one market; sell to buyers in another (or coordinate transactions between them); capture spread, commission, facilitation fee, or value-added service margin; repeat at scale with optimized working capital and risk management.

---

## Current Stage & Market Context (2025–2026)

**Market Backdrop:**
- Global goods trade contracted in 2024 but rebounded with 3.3% growth expected in 2025, driven by Asian and emerging markets (India, Vietnam, Indonesia, Philippines).
- Commodity prices declining (projected 7% drop in both 2025 and 2026) due to weak global growth and policy uncertainty—but precious metals (+5% expected in 2026) and industrial metals (copper, aluminum, nickel) show potential due to infrastructure and green energy demand.
- Energy sector facing 12% price decline in 2025, 10% in 2026.
- Geopolitical uncertainty and tariff volatility remain the primary disruptors.

**Strategic Context:**
- Regionalization of supply chains is accelerating (companies building "near-shore" and "friend-shore" networks instead of pure cost optimization).
- Service-based value models (consulting, logistics coordination, compliance, financing facilitation) are becoming as important as commodity spreads.
- Fintech and digitalization disrupting trade finance: blockchain-based letters of credit, supply chain financing platforms, and data-driven logistics creating both opportunities and competitive threats.
- Mid-market and niche traders are consolidating or finding specialized niches; generalists face margin pressure.

## Business Model Breakdown — Revenue Streams

Trading companies generate revenue through multiple levers. Not all apply equally; your mix depends on market positioning and capital availability.

### 1. Commodity/Goods Spread (Core Margin)
- **Model:** Buy low, sell high; keep the difference.
- **Typical Margin:** 3–7% for commodities; up to 12–15% for specialty/manufactured goods.
- **Dependencies:** Capital to hold inventory, access to better-priced sources, fast execution to avoid price moves.
- **Risk:** Price volatility, inventory obsolescence, FX fluctuations, logistics delays eating margin.
- **Note:** Tight margins require volume; low volume + tight margins = unprofitable.

### 2. Commission (Agency Model)
- **Model:** Connect buyer and seller; take a percentage of transaction value without holding title.
- **Typical Rate:** 1–3% for commodities; 3–8% for specialty goods; 5–10% for consulting/sourcing.
- **Dependencies:** Trust network, market knowledge, deal flow access.
- **Risk:** Lower per-deal revenue, but also lower capital lock-up and inventory risk.
- **Advantage:** Scalable once reputation is built; can handle multiple transaction types simultaneously.

### 3. Trade Facilitation / Logistics Fees
- **Model:** Handle customs, shipping, documentation (bills of lading, packing lists, certificates of origin, letters of credit).
- **Typical Fee:** $500–$5,000 per shipment depending on complexity; can be percentage-based or fixed.
- **Dependencies:** Compliance expertise, relationships with customs brokers, shipping partners, banks; understanding of Incoterms and trade agreements.
- **Risk:** Regulatory changes can render processes obsolete; liability for errors.
- **Opportunity:** Sticky revenue stream if you become the trusted logistics coordinator.

### 4. Supply Chain Finance / Working Capital Solutions
- **Model:** Act as intermediary or guarantor; help suppliers get paid faster (against receivables), help buyers extend payment terms.
- **Typical Fee:** 1–3% per transaction, often from both sides.
- **Dependencies:** Bank relationships, balance sheet strength (to back guarantees), cash reserves.
- **Market Context:** Global trade finance market is USD 53.8B (2025) and growing 6.1% annually; supply chain finance expected to reach USD 11.52B by 2030 (up from USD 7.58B in 2025).
- **Risk:** If a buyer or supplier defaults, you may absorb the loss; requires strict credit vetting.
- **Regulatory:** Letters of credit are shifting to digital (blockchain, e-LC platforms); early movers have cost advantage.

### 5. Consulting / Sourcing Services
- **Model:** Fee-based advisory: market research, supplier vetting, compliance reviews, tariff/trade agreement analysis, landed cost modeling.
- **Typical Fee:** $5,000–$50,000+ per project, or retainer model (3–5% of transaction value ongoing).
- **Dependencies:** Deep domain expertise, ability to deliver data and insights competitors don't have.
- **Differentiation:** High margin (60–80% if you scale delivery), defensible (hard to commoditize expertise).
- **Market Context:** Import/export management companies (EMCs) have successfully positioned as outsourced export departments for mid-sized manufacturers—this is essentially a consultancy + commission hybrid.

### 6. Payment Risk / Financing (Capital-Intensive)
- **Model:** Offer buyer credit (you finance their purchase); take a return in the form of interest or spread.
- **Typical Return:** 4–10% depending on buyer risk and terms (30–180 days).
- **Dependencies:** Capital availability, credit underwriting capability, legal framework for securing the transaction.
- **Risk:** High—if the buyer fails, you lose capital. Requires strict vetting and possibly collateral.
- **Note:** Smaller trading companies typically avoid this unless they have specific capital or are integrated with a financing partner.

## Growth Levers (Prioritized)

### Tier 1 — Immediate Impact (Do First)
1. **Build Repeatable, High-Margin Sourcing Channels**
   - Identify 3–5 specific commodity/product categories where you have supplier access competitors don't.
   - Lock in exclusive relationships (even informal ones) with key suppliers.
   - Validate that you can move volume at profitable spreads; "one-off great deals" don't scale.

2. **Develop a Trusted Network (Buyer & Supplier)**
   - Every trade deal depends on relationships. Website, certifications, case studies, and referrals matter more here than in other industries.
   - Early revenue will come from existing networks—activate them first before spending on lead gen.
   - Document proof of successful prior trades (anonymized if needed) to build credibility with new partners.

3. **Master Trade Compliance & Documentation**
   - Tariffs, sanctions lists, export controls, certificates of origin, Incoterms—errors here kill deals and create legal risk.
   - Build internal capability or partner with a customs broker you trust and can scale with.
   - Compliance expertise becomes a moat: buyers and suppliers will trust you more if you handle the regulatory burden.

### Tier 2 — Scale & Diversification (Do Once Volume Proves)
4. **Add Service Layers (Commission + Logistics + Consulting)**
   - Once you have transaction volume, layer on logistics coordination, payment facilitation, consulting on tariffs/landing costs.
   - Each service layer adds 1–3% margin without adding commodity risk.
   - Sticky revenue: if you're the trusted coordinator, customers stay.

5. **Expand Product Categories or Trade Routes**
   - After proving a sourcing channel works, replicate it in adjacent categories or routes.
   - Example: If you source electronics from Vietnam, add semiconductors, then expand to Thailand.
   - Monitor margin by route/product—kill low-margin channels, scale high-margin ones.

6. **Develop a Vertically Integrated Niche**
   - Instead of trading everything, become *the* expert in one vertical (e.g., organic agricultural commodities to Southeast Asia; industrial metals to automotive suppliers; specialized chemicals to pharmaceutical manufacturers).
   - Vertical specialization commands better margins (10–15% vs. 3–5% for generalists) because customers pay for your specific expertise.

### Tier 3 — Leverage & Growth (Once Scale Is Proven)
7. **Build a Supply Chain Finance Capability**
   - Offer working capital solutions (buyer credit, supplier financing) to lock in recurring revenue.
   - Requires capital and bank partnerships; typically only viable once you have proven transaction history and volume.
   - Example: You finance a buyer's 60-day purchase at 1.5% fee; you recover capital + fee within 60 days.

8. **Move Toward Higher-Margin Services (Consulting, Trading Advisory)**
   - As you develop market expertise, shift from transaction-based trading toward advisory (landed cost modeling, tariff optimization, supply chain redesign).
   - Higher margin (60–80%), lower capital tie-up, more defensible competitive position.

9. **Technology/Platform Play**
   - Once you have transaction data, market relationships, and compliance expertise, consider building or licensing a platform (e.g., digital RFQ, supply chain visibility, compliance dashboard).
   - Highest leverage but highest risk and capital requirement; typically a 3–5 year play.

## Competitive Landscape

### Large Integrated Trading Houses (Tier 1)
- **Examples:** Glencore, Trafigura, Vitol, Cargill, ADM, Bunge, Louis Dreyfus
- **Characteristics:** 
  - Global footprint, diversified commodities, own logistics and financing.
  - Billions in annual revenue; thousands of employees.
  - Capital-heavy; integrate trading, production, logistics, and finance vertically.
- **How They Win:** Scale, capital access, proprietary data, political connections, integrated supply chains.
- **Your Competitive Advantage:** You cannot compete on scale or capital. You compete on agility, niche expertise, and relationships in underserved markets/products.

### Mid-Market Regional Traders (Tier 2)
- **Examples:** Mercuria, Gunvor, Olam, Marubeni, COFCO (in select regions)
- **Characteristics:**
  - USD 1–10B annual revenue, 100–1000s of employees.
  - Focused on 2–3 commodity types or geographic regions.
  - Often have access to development bank finance, structured credit, or state backing (in some cases).
- **How They Win:** Regional expertise, established buyer/seller networks, relationship depth, mid-market credit access.
- **Your Position:** You compete against them in narrow niches—either geographic (e.g., "the importer to know in East Africa") or product-specific ("the guy who sources fair-trade cocoa").

### Niche & Specialist Traders (Your Tier)
- **Examples:** ED&F Man (coffee), countless single-product or single-route traders
- **Characteristics:**
  - USD 10M–500M annual revenue; 10–100 employees.
  - Deep expertise in one product, route, or customer segment.
  - Tight relationships with suppliers and buyers; often founder-led.
- **How They Win:** Trust, specialized knowledge, agility, personal relationships.
- **Threats:** Scale competitors entering the niche; margin compression from fintech/platforms; access to credit drying up.

### Emerging Competition: Export Management Companies (EMCs)
- **Business Model:** Act as outsourced export department for mid-sized manufacturers (don't hold inventory; earn commission or fee).
- **Growth:** This model is growing as manufacturers want to enter export markets without building in-house capability.
- **Relevance:** If you position as an EMC rather than a pure trader, you reduce capital requirements and inventory risk—but also lower per-transaction upside.

### New Competitive Threats (2025–2026)
1. **Fintech / Digital Trade Platforms**
   - Blockchain-based letters of credit, automated RFQ matching, real-time compliance checking.
   - Threat: They disintermediate traders by directly connecting buyer and seller.
   - Opportunity: If you don't integrate with these platforms, you become slower and more expensive than platforms.

2. **State-Backed or SOE Traders (Especially in Asia)**
   - COFCO (China), and others are growing aggressively, backed by government capital and policy support.
   - They can absorb lower margins and are willing to trade on non-commercial terms (as strategic objectives).
   - Threat: They can out-bid on key commodity flows; hard to compete on price.

3. **Supply Chain Optimization Software**
   - Companies like Fourkites, Everstream, Coupa, and others are helping large buyers source and coordinate logistics directly.
   - Threat: Reduces buyer reliance on trader as "logistics coordinator."
   - Opportunity: Integrate with (or partner with) these platforms to remain relevant.

## CEO Decision Framework

Before any significant decision (new product line, geographic expansion, hire, capital spend, pricing change, partnership), ask:

### Business Model Questions
- **What revenue stream does this move activate?** (Spread, commission, service fee, financing, consulting?)
- **What's the margin, and how many transactions do we need to break even?** If you don't know the unit economics, don't do it.
- **Does this fit our niche or dilute it?** Generalists in trading face margin compression. Stay disciplined about scope.
- **Can this scale with our current capital structure?** If it requires 3x more working capital and you don't have it, it's a no.

### Competitive & Market Questions
- **Who else does this, and why would a customer choose us?** If 10 competitors offer the same service, don't enter unless you have a clear cost or relationship advantage.
- **Is the market growing or consolidating?** Consolidating markets favor large players; growing markets favor specialists with fast execution.
- **What tariffs, export controls, or compliance risks apply?** One regulatory miss can destroy the deal. Non-negotiable to verify before committing capital.

### Customer & Execution Questions
- **Who is the actual buyer, and do they already trust us?** Existing relationships close faster and cheaper than greenfield.
- **Can we deliver better/faster/cheaper than they expect?** Set expectations you can exceed. Missed expectations kill trust faster than anything.
- **What happens if something goes wrong?** (Payment default, shipment damage, regulatory issue, price collapse.) Do we have a plan? Do we have insurance or collateral?

### Financial & Risk Questions
- **What's the payback period? Is it faster than our cost of capital?** If a trade takes 6 months and costs 5% in financing, margin must be >7.5% to make sense.
- **What's the downside?** If you hold inventory and prices fall 10%, can you absorb it without threatening the business?
- **Are we taking on credit risk, FX risk, or price risk we don't understand?** If yes, hedge it or don't do it.

### Team & Execution Questions
- **Do we have the capability to execute this?** Compliance, logistics, customs, documentation, credit vetting—these aren't soft skills.
- **Is this a distraction from what's working?** Opportunity cost is real. New initiatives should only happen if they're higher-return than scaling what's already working.

## Things to Push Back On

If someone (including Alex) proposes these, challenge it:

### "We should trade everything and be a one-stop shop."
- **Reality:** Generalist traders face margin compression and high complexity. You'll be outcompeted by both large integrated houses (who have scale) and specialists (who have depth).
- **Push Back:** Identify 2–3 product categories where we have real advantage (supplier access, expertise, existing relationships). Build depth in those first. Expand later only if margin stays healthy.

### "We should focus on margins; volume doesn't matter."
- **Reality:** In trading, you need enough volume to cover fixed costs (team, compliance, infrastructure). One trade a month won't pay salaries.
- **Push Back:** Model the unit economics. How many transactions at this margin does it take to hit profitability? Do we have the pipeline to hit that volume within 12 months?

### "We should match Glencore's price to win deals."
- **Reality:** You can't compete on price against traders with 10x your capital and cost structure. You'll lose money, not win.
- **Push Back:** We don't win on price. We win on trust, speed, or niche expertise. Price the deal so that *we* can afford to deliver exceptional service.

### "Let's build a fintech platform for trading."
- **Reality:** Capital-intensive, long development cycle, uncertain ROI. Delays core business focus. You're a trader, not a software company.
- **Push Back:** Don't build. Partner or integrate with existing platforms (blockchain LC platforms, digital RFQ platforms, compliance software) and use them to stay competitive. If you see a genuine market gap after 2+ years of operating, revisit.

### "We should offer buyer financing to close deals."
- **Reality:** You're adding credit risk and capital lock-up without your core competency (trading/sourcing). Buyers default. You lose capital.
- **Push Back:** Only offer financing if: (a) you have capital reserves specifically allocated for this, (b) you have credit vetting expertise, and (c) buyers are willing to pay a premium (5%+ fee) for the service. Otherwise, refer them to a bank.

### "We'll go after every RFQ and try to win based on price."
- **Reality:** Low-margin deals destroy profitability. Every deal must meet your minimum margin or you're wasting time.
- **Push Back:** Set a minimum margin threshold (e.g., 5% spread or 2% commission). Walk away from anything lower unless it's a relationship-building exception and you can afford it.

### "Let's hire a sales team to cold-call potential buyers."
- **Reality:** In trading, deals come from relationships, referrals, and reputation. Cold outbound has low ROI. Cold email gets ignored.
- **Push Back:** Invest in building case studies and credibility on the website first. Activate existing networks. Referral incentives for customers who bring in new deals. Once you have proof of concept, *then* consider a more active business development approach.

### "We should operate in 10 different markets/regions simultaneously."
- **Reality:** You'll have no deep relationships in any of them. Execution will suffer. Compliance risk explodes.
- **Push Back:** Pick 2–3 key routes (e.g., Vietnam → US, Ghana → Europe, China → LATAM). Build relationships, prove the model, then expand to 2–3 more. Depth first, breadth later.

## Non-Negotiables

These are hard constraints. Violating them will damage the business long-term:

### 1. Trade Compliance (Non-Negotiable)
- Every transaction must be vetted against:
  - Sanctions lists (OFAC, EU, UN, etc.)
  - Export controls (especially if trading to sanctioned countries or sensitive goods like chemicals, electronics, dual-use items)
  - Tariff classifications (HS codes, origin rules)
  - Customs documentation requirements (bills of lading, packing lists, certificates of origin, commercial invoices, sometimes letters of credit)
  - Industry-specific regulations (food/agricultural: phytosanitary certs; chemicals: safety data sheets; metals: conflict minerals; etc.)
- **Non-Negotiable Rule:** If there is ANY doubt about compliance, do not execute. Consult a trade lawyer or customs broker.
- **Risk:** Penalties, debarment from export, reputational damage, personal liability (in some cases).
- **Responsibility:** Make someone on the team (or external partner) explicitly accountable for compliance vetting on every deal.

### 2. Use Correct Incoterms & Shipping Terms
- Define every transaction with explicit Incoterms (FOB, CIF, EXW, DDP, etc.) and insurance requirements.
- Don't assume—many deals fail because parties misunderstood who pays for shipping, insurance, duties, or risk transfer.
- **Non-Negotiable Rule:** All contracts and invoices must specify Incoterms and shipping terms explicitly. If a customer or supplier resists, that's a red flag.

### 3. Credit Vetting & Payment Risk Management
- Before extending credit (whether 30-day payment terms or financing), verify:
  - Buyer's creditworthiness (financial statements, bank reference, trade references, D&B report)
  - Buyer's payment history with you (if repeat customer)
  - Appropriate collateral, guarantees, or insurance (e.g., letters of credit from a bank)
- **Non-Negotiable Rule:** Don't offer net-30 or net-60 payment terms to anyone without vetting. The extra few percentage points of margin aren't worth a default.
- **Fallback:** Use letters of credit, documentary collections, or prepayment for unproven buyers.

### 4. Accurate Landed Cost Modeling
- Before quoting a price, calculate the true delivered cost:
  - COG (cost of goods)
  - Shipping, freight, insurance
  - Tariffs and duties
  - Customs and documentation fees
  - Your margin
  - FX exposure (if buying in one currency, selling in another)
- **Non-Negotiable Rule:** If you can't confidently model the landed cost within ±5%, don't quote.
- **Risk:** Bad modeling means you thought you made 5% margin and actually lost 3%.

### 5. Clear Written Contracts
- Every deal >$10K should have a written contract (or at least a detailed email exchange confirming terms).
- Specify:
  - Quantity, quality (specs, inspection rights), price
  - Incoterms and shipping terms
  - Payment terms, currency, FX clause (if applicable)
  - Delivery date and location
  - Liability limits, insurance, dispute resolution
  - Termination conditions
- **Non-Negotiable Rule:** Ambiguous contracts lead to disputes. Disputes destroy margin and relationships.

### 6. Regulatory & Legal Review Before Market Entry
- Before entering a new country or product category, verify:
  - Import/export licensing requirements
  - Tariff classifications and trade agreement benefits (e.g., USMCA, GSP)
  - Compliance certifications or registrations required
  - Dispute resolution and legal framework (What courts have jurisdiction if a deal goes wrong?)
- **Non-Negotiable Rule:** Consult a trade lawyer or customs broker for new routes. One compliance miss can kill a deal and expose you to penalties.

### 7. No Undisclosed Side Deals or Off-Books Transactions
- All revenue streams and transactions must flow through the accounting system.
- Why: Undisclosed deals hide true profitability, create tax risk, and prevent you from scaling (you can't manage what you don't measure).
- **Non-Negotiable Rule:** 100% transaction visibility. Every deal logged, every margin tracked.

## Working Capital & Capital Requirements

Trading is capital-intensive. You need enough cash to:
1. **Pay suppliers before you get paid by buyers.** (This is the working capital cycle.)
2. **Hold inventory** during shipment, customs clearance, and buyer takeover.
3. **Weather disruptions** (buyer delays, customs holds, price swings, payment defaults).

### Typical Working Capital Model
- **Small trader (USD 1–5M annual revenue):**
  - Operating capital: USD 200K–USD 500K
  - This allows you to manage 30–60 day float between paying suppliers and collecting from buyers.
  - Most rely on bank lines of credit, supplier terms (net-30/45), or buyer prepayment.

- **Mid-market trader (USD 10–50M annual revenue):**
  - Operating capital: USD 1–5M
  - Larger capital cushion allows you to hold inventory, offer better payment terms, and invest in infrastructure.
  - Access to more sophisticated financing: working capital lines, supply chain finance, trade credit insurance.

### Financing Options
1. **Bank Working Capital Lines:** Traditional; requires strong financials and bank relationships. Cost: 4–7% annual interest + fees.
2. **Supply Chain Financing:** Factor invoices or get paid early against receivables. Cost: 1–3% per transaction.
3. **Trade Credit Insurance:** Protect against buyer default. Cost: 0.5–2% of transaction value depending on buyer credit rating.
4. **Supplier Terms:** If you have good relationships, negotiate net-30 or net-45 from suppliers. This reduces your working capital need.
5. **Customer Prepayment:** Best case—get paid upfront. Requires trust or offers a small discount.

## Key Metrics to Track

These should be reviewed monthly:

1. **Gross Margin %:** (Revenue – COGS) / Revenue. Target: 8–12% for commodity trading; 15–25% for specialty/value-added.
2. **Average Deal Size:** Total revenue / number of deals. If declining, you're doing smaller, harder deals.
3. **Transaction Velocity:** Average days from order to payment. Target: <90 days. High velocity = lower working capital need.
4. **Bad Debt / Default Rate:** Losses from non-payment / total transaction value. Target: <0.5%. Higher = credit vetting issues.
5. **Revenue per Employee:** Total revenue / headcount. If declining, you're not scaling; you're just adding overhead.
6. **Working Capital as % of Revenue:** (Inventory + Receivables – Payables) / Annual Revenue. Target: <20%. Higher = inefficient capital use.
7. **Customer Concentration:** Top 5 customers as % of revenue. Target: <40%. High concentration = risk if one customer leaves.

## Strategic Priorities (Next 12 Months)

1. **Validate the niche.** Document 5–10 completed transactions (even small ones) in your chosen category/route. Confirm margin, time-to-close, customer satisfaction.
2. **Build the website as a credibility asset.** Case studies, certifications, clear value prop. International traders judge you on professionalism from day one.
3. **Establish compliance infrastructure.** Customs broker relationship, legal review template, sanctions screening process.
4. **Prove the sourcing advantage.** Lock in 2–3 key suppliers who offer you better pricing or exclusive access than competitors.
5. **Activate the network.** Referrals and existing relationships will fund 50%+ of early revenue. Build relationships systematically.
